Introduction to Computational Thinking

Computers are fast, but they are not clever. They only know how to follow steps. This week, you'll learn the four moves a programmer uses to turn any messy real-world problem into something a computer can actually solve.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

1
Define computational thinking and name its four pillars: decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, and algorithm design.
2
Decompose a real-world problem into smaller, manageable parts that can be solved one at a time.
3
Identify patterns and apply abstraction to focus on the details that matter and ignore the ones that don't.
4
Write a simple algorithm as numbered steps and as pseudocode — the bridge between plain English and real code.
01
Section 1 · What Is Computational Thinking?

Thinking like a problem-solver, not a computer

Imagine you walked into the kitchen and asked a robot to make you a peanut-butter sandwich. The robot is fast, strong, and follows orders perfectly — but it has never made one before. If you said "just make me a sandwich," it would freeze. It needs every step spelled out: open the bag, pull out one slice, set it on the plate, pick up the knife…

That is what programming is like. Computers don't understand problems — they only execute steps you give them. Computational thinking is the skill of taking a real-world problem and reshaping it into a form a computer (or any careful step-follower) can actually solve.

Computational thinking has four pillars. You'll meet them in the sections below — and then use them in everything you build for the rest of this course.

📘 Vocabulary

Computational thinking — a way of solving problems by breaking them down, spotting patterns, focusing on what matters, and writing clear steps.

Problem — the gap between where you are and where you want to be. In computing, the problem is what you're trying to make the computer do.

Pillar — one of the four core skills of computational thinking; you usually need all four together.

02
Section 2 · Pillar 1 — Decomposition

Big problems are just small problems stacked up

Your friend tells you, "Help me plan my birthday party!" If you tried to solve that sentence in one move, your brain would short-circuit. There are too many decisions hiding inside it.

So you do what every good problem-solver does — you split it into decomposition: smaller pieces you can actually answer one at a time.

1
Who is coming?
Make the guest list. This is its own little problem — names, contact info, max number.
2
Where will it happen?
Backyard? Bowling alley? Pick a spot, then figure out how to get everyone there.
3
What will you eat?
Pizza? Tacos? Cake flavor? Each food choice is a tiny decision of its own.
4
What will you actually do?
Games, music, movie? Pick one and plan it. Then pick the next one.

"Plan a birthday party" sounded gigantic. Once you decompose it into guests, place, food, activities, each piece is solvable in a few minutes. That's the whole magic of decomposition: it turns the impossible into a checklist.

Programmers use the same trick every day. Building a video game? Decompose it into: player movement, enemies, scoring, levels, sounds, save system. Each piece becomes its own small program — and the whole thing becomes possible.

✅ Check for Understanding

Pause and try: You've been asked to "make the school cafeteria work better." That's a giant problem. Decompose it into at least four smaller problems someone could actually start working on.

(Hint: think about lines, food choices, seating, cleanup…)

Section 3 · Pillar 2 — Pattern Recognition

If you've solved one, you can solve a hundred

Here's a math word problem: "Maria has 12 apples and gives 4 to her brother. How many does she have left?" Easy — 8.

Here's another: "A jar holds 30 marbles. Tomás takes out 11. How many remain?" Easy — 19.

The numbers are different. The story is different. But the pattern is identical: start amount − amount removed = answer. Once you've spotted the pattern, you can solve every problem of that shape — even ones you've never seen before.

That move is called pattern recognition, and it is everywhere in computer science. Why? Because computers are champions at doing the same thing over and over. If you can describe the pattern once, the computer can run it on a million inputs without breaking a sweat.

🔍 Same pattern
Different stories, same shape
  • "How many minutes until 3:00?" — same as "subtract current time"
  • "Sort my contacts" — same as "sort my songs"
  • "Find the cheapest sneaker under $50" — same as "find the cheapest pizza"
⚡ Why it matters
Reuse beats reinventing
  • One pattern → many problems solved
  • Less code, fewer mistakes
  • You spot what's really being asked
  • The basis of every loop and function you'll write
🌎 Real-World Connection

Doctors use pattern recognition every shift. When a patient describes symptoms — fever, sore throat, swollen glands — the doctor isn't seeing it for the first time. They've seen the shape of that combination before, and recognizing the pattern lets them act fast.

Programmers, doctors, mechanics, and chess masters all share this superpower: they've trained their brains to see the shape underneath the surface details.

Section 4 · Pillar 3 — Abstraction

Throw out the noise, keep the signal

Pull up a subway map on your phone. The lines are perfectly straight. The stations are evenly spaced. The whole city is squished into one neat diagram.

That map is a lie — and on purpose. A real subway tunnel curves and dips and runs different distances between stops. But for someone trying to get from Station A to Station B, none of that matters. What matters is: which line do I take, and where do I switch? The map throws away every detail that doesn't help, and keeps only the ones that do.

That move is called abstraction — keeping what matters for this problem, and ignoring the rest.

You already use abstraction every day. When you ask a friend, "Want to come over?" you abstract away their address, their bus schedule, their parents' work hours — you focus on the one thing that matters for the question: yes or no?

In programming, abstraction means: when you write a chunk of code called add_to_cart, the rest of your program doesn't need to know how it works inside. It only needs to know what to put in and what to expect out. The messy details stay hidden.

💭 Think About It

An emoji is abstraction in your pocket. The 😂 face stands in for "I think that's hilarious," "I'm laughing nervously," or "I want to keep this conversation light." A real human face has thousands of tiny details — eyebrows, wrinkles, eye color — and the emoji throws nearly all of it away. Yet you understand it instantly.

Where else have you noticed abstraction making complicated things simple today?

Section 5 · Pillar 4 — Algorithms & Pseudocode

Step-by-step: the language of getting things done

Once you've decomposed the problem, recognized its pattern, and abstracted away the noise, the last move is to write down a clear set of steps. That's an algorithm — a finite list of instructions that, if followed exactly, solves the problem.

You already follow algorithms all the time. A recipe is an algorithm. Brushing your teeth is an algorithm. The morning routine to get out the door for school is an algorithm. They all share the same four traits:

📥 Input
What you start with — ingredients, numbers, a player's name.
⚙️ Steps
A clear, ordered list. No skipping. No guessing.
📤 Output
What you end up with — the cake, the answer, the high score.
🛑 Finite
It eventually stops. An algorithm that runs forever is broken.

    💡 Did You Know?

    The word algorithm comes from the name of a 9th-century Persian mathematician — al-Khwārizmī — who wrote one of the first systematic books on solving problems with steps. His name was Latinized into Algorithmi, and the word stuck for over a thousand years.

    Every time you say "algorithm," you're quietly honoring a scholar who lived more than 1,200 years before the first computer was even imagined.

    📚 Key Vocabulary — Week 19

    Computational thinking
    A way of solving problems by decomposing them, finding patterns, abstracting essentials, and writing clear steps.
    Decomposition
    Breaking a big problem into smaller parts that can each be solved on their own.
    Pattern recognition
    Spotting similarities between different problems so you can reuse a solution that already works.
    Abstraction
    Keeping the details that matter for the problem and ignoring the ones that don't.
    Algorithm
    A finite, ordered list of steps that solves a problem when followed exactly.
    Pseudocode
    A plain-English sketch of an algorithm — easy for humans to read, not yet a real programming language.
    Input
    The information an algorithm starts with — like the numbers, names, or text you feed in.
    Output
    The result an algorithm produces after the steps are followed — the answer, the picture, the score.

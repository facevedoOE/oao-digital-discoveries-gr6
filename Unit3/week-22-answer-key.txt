Q1. In technology, what does ETHICS mostly mean?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Knowing how to fix a broken computer
     ❌ That's tech support, not ethics. Ethics is about
        right and wrong, not hardware repair.
  B) Thinking about what is right and fair when our tech
     choices affect other people                                ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Ethics is the careful thinking we do about how our
        technology choices treat others — privacy, honesty,
        fairness, respect. It's the heart of digital
        citizenship. (SC.6.PE.2.1)
  C) Memorizing all the rules of one programming language
     ❌ That's syntax, not ethics. Programming languages
        have rules — but ethics is about how we use them.
  D) Building the fastest possible processor
     ❌ That's hardware engineering. Ethics is a thinking
        skill, not a speed competition.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q2. Which of the following is the BEST example of AUTOMATION?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) A human cashier scanning items by hand
     ❌ A human doing the work IS the work — that's not
        automation. Automation replaces or assists with
        the manual task.
  B) A teacher grading every essay personally
     ❌ A human grading by hand is the opposite of
        automation. (Software that auto-flags spelling
        errors WOULD be automation — but that's not the
        option here.)
  C) A self-checkout lane at the grocery store that scans
     items and takes payment with no cashier                    ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Self-checkout is a textbook automation example: a
        machine handles a task that a human used to do —
        scanning, totaling, and accepting payment.
        (SC.6.PE.1.2)
  D) A friend texting you a reminder
     ❌ A friend (a human) sending a text is regular
        communication, not automation.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q3. What does the term DIGITAL DIVIDE describe?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) The split between Apple and Android phone users
     ❌ That's a brand preference, not a divide of
        opportunity.
  B) The difference between websites and mobile apps
     ❌ That's a tech-format difference, not a fairness
        gap.
  C) The gap between people who have fast, reliable
     internet and modern devices and people who don't —
     and the unfair effects that gap creates                    ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ The digital divide is about access. When school,
        work, healthcare, and opportunity all happen
        online, lacking reliable internet means lacking
        opportunity. (SC.6.PE.1.3)
  D) The line between online games and real-life sports
     ❌ Game vs sport is a personal hobby choice, not a
        societal access gap.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q4. Which of these is an ACCESSIBILITY tool?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) A graphics card that makes games run faster
     ❌ A faster GPU is a performance upgrade, not an
        accessibility tool.
  B) A high-end gaming mouse with extra buttons
     ❌ A premium mouse is a comfort/style upgrade, not
        a tool designed for users with different
        abilities.
  C) A 4K monitor for sharper movies
     ❌ A nicer display is a quality upgrade — not an
        accessibility-specific feature.
  D) A screen reader that reads text aloud for people
     who are blind or have low vision                           ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Screen readers are core accessibility technology.
        Captions, voice control, and adjustable text size
        are other classic examples that make tech usable
        for more people. (SC.6.PE.1.3)

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q5. Which statement BEST describes the relationship between
    technology and society?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Technology controls society — society has no say
     ❌ That's only half right. Yes, tech shapes how we
        live — but society pushes back through laws,
        purchases, and choices.
  B) Society controls technology — technology has no
     effect on us
     ❌ Also only half right. Society does shape tech —
        but tech genuinely changes daily life (look at
        the smartphone in your pocket).
  C) Technology and society shape each other in a
     two-way relationship: tech changes how we live,
     and our needs, laws, and choices change tech back          ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ This is the heart of Week 22. Tech and society
        are in a constant loop: an invention reshapes
        daily life, and society responds with new
        habits, laws, and demands that reshape the
        next invention. (SC.6.PE.2.1)
  D) Technology and society have nothing to do with
     each other
     ❌ That's the opposite of true. They are deeply
        linked — every technology is invented by, used
        by, and shaped by people.

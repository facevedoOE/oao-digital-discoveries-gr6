Decomposition: Breaking Big Problems Down

"Plan a birthday party." "Build a video game." "Clean the whole house." Some problems feel impossibly big — until you slice them into pieces small enough to actually solve. That's decomposition, the second pillar of computational thinking, and it's the single most powerful trick programmers use to tackle hard work.

What is decomposition?

Last week you met algorithms — clear, ordered, step-by-step recipes. But what do you do when the problem is too big to write a recipe for? You can't write a 10,000-step recipe for "build a video game" all at once. Your brain doesn't work that way, and a computer programmer's brain doesn't either.

That's where the second pillar of computational thinking comes in. Decomposition is the act of taking one big problem and breaking it into smaller sub-problems — each one small enough that you (or a computer) can actually solve it. Then you solve the small ones one at a time, and the big one is solved automatically.

It sounds simple. It is simple. But it's also the secret behind every app on your phone, every level in every video game, every movie special effect, every robot, and every search you've ever typed into Google. None of those exist without decomposition.

You already do this — every day

When you "get ready for school" each morning, you don't think of it as one giant task. You break it into smaller ones without realizing: get up, brush teeth, eat breakfast, get dressed, pack bag, leave. That's decomposition. Six small problems, each easy. Strung together, they solve the giant one.

Look at how three different "big problems" decompose:

🎮 "Build a video game"
→ design characters · build levels · write rules · add sound · test for bugs · publish
🍕 "Make dinner for 10 people"
→ pick the menu · check what's in the fridge · go shopping · prep ingredients · cook · serve
🌎 "Write a book report"
→ pick the book · read it · take notes · plan the report · write the draft · revise · turn it in

Notice the pattern: a vague, scary "big problem" turns into a list of small, specific tasks. Each task is something you could write an algorithm for. That's the magic.

💡 Did You Know?

When NASA flew astronauts to the moon in 1969, the entire mission was decomposed into thousands of sub-problems by a team of programmers — including Margaret Hamilton, who led the team that wrote the Apollo flight software. Their decomposed code was so reliable that when the lunar lander's computer started crashing during landing, the software automatically dropped the unimportant sub-problems and kept the critical ones running. The astronauts landed safely. Decomposition didn't just send people to the moon — it brought them home.

How to decompose — the four-step move

There's a reliable way to decompose any big problem. Practice it on small things first, and it'll save you on big things later.

STEP 1 Write down the big problem in one sentence.
If you can't say it in one sentence, you don't really understand it yet. "Make my room look great for a sleepover."
STEP 2 List the sub-problems you'd need to solve.
Don't worry about order yet — just list them. Clean up · move furniture · set up the snacks · plan the games · pick the music.
STEP 3 Check each piece — is it small enough to solve?
If a piece still feels overwhelming, decompose it again. "Clean up" might split into pick up clothes · vacuum · make bed · clear desk.
STEP 4 Solve the small pieces, one at a time.
Now each piece has a clear algorithm. The big problem solves itself as you finish the small ones.

When do you stop decomposing?

Stop when each sub-problem is small enough that you could write a clear algorithm for it — without breaking it down again. That's the test. If a piece still feels like "I'd need to think about that for a while," it's still too big. Cut it down once more.

A good rule of thumb: each piece should be something you could solve in a few minutes, or write 5–10 algorithm steps for. If a sub-problem still feels like a project, it isn't decomposed enough.

⚖️ Florida Standard Spotlight

SC.K12.CTR.2.1 — Create representations of digital problems.

Decomposing a big problem isn't just about doing it in your head — it's about showing it on paper, screen, or whiteboard. The most common visual is a tree: the big problem on top, sub-problems branching down, with each branch decomposed further if it's still too big. Diagrams like this turn invisible thinking into something you and your team can actually look at, edit, and improve. That's why every CS classroom in Florida teaches representations alongside the thinking itself.

Decomposition + Algorithms = Code

Here's why these two pillars belong together. Decomposition tells you what to solve. Algorithms tell you how to solve each piece. You can't really do one without the other.

Think back to last week's PB&J algorithm. That was already a decomposition. "Make a sandwich" got broken into: get bread · get peanut butter · get a knife · open jar · scoop · spread · stack · slice. Then each of those tiny pieces became a clear, ordered step in the algorithm.

When you start writing real Python code in Week 22, this is exactly how you'll work: decompose the program into small pieces, write a tiny algorithm for each piece, then translate each algorithm into code. Big software is built from many small algorithms — and decomposition is what tells you where one algorithm ends and the next one begins.

📘 Key Vocabulary

Decomposition
Breaking a big problem into smaller, easier sub-problems you can actually solve.
Sub-problem
One smaller piece that comes out of decomposing a bigger problem.
Decomposition tree
A diagram showing the big problem on top with sub-problems branching down — a way to see your decomposition.
Top-down design
A way of designing programs that starts with the big goal and decomposes downward into smaller pieces.
Module
A small, complete piece of a larger program — usually the result of decomposing well.
Pattern recognition
Spotting what repeats across sub-problems. The third pillar of computational thinking — coming next week.

💭 Weekly Reflection — Decompose Your Weekend

Pick one big task you want to get done this weekend. It can be anything — clean your room, finish a project, learn a new game, plan something for friends.

Now decompose it using the four-step move from this lesson:

  1. Write the big problem in one sentence.
  2. List 4–6 sub-problems.
  3. Check each one — if any still feels too big, break it down again.
  4. Mark the order you'll tackle them in.

Bring your decomposition to class — you'll use it as the input to next week's lesson on patterns.

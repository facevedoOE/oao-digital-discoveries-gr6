UNIT 3 • WEEK 22 • READING

Science, Technology & Society — A Two-Way Street

Technology shapes the way you live, talk, learn, and play. But here's the part most people miss: society shapes technology right back. This week you'll trace that two-way conversation — across centuries of inventions, into your own pocket, and out toward the choices your generation will make next.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

1
Explain the two-way relationship between technology and society — how each one constantly reshapes the other.
2
Trace key inventions from the printing press to modern AI and describe how each one changed daily life.
3
Analyze impacts of automation, accessibility, and the digital divide on real people and communities.
4
Apply ethical thinking to questions about privacy, sustainability, and digital citizenship in everyday tech use.
01
Section 1 · The Two-Way Street

Tech changes us — and we change tech right back

Pull out your phone for a second. (Yes, mentally pull it out — keep it in your pocket.) Think about what it lets you do that your great-grandparents could not have imagined: video-call a cousin overseas, ask a question and get an answer in two seconds, watch a friend's birthday from your couch, learn a guitar riff at 3 a.m.

That little rectangle is a technology — a tool people invented to solve a problem. And it has reshaped almost everything about how you live: how you talk to friends, how you find directions, how you remember things, even how you fall asleep.

But here's the trick most people miss: society didn't just receive that phone. Society asked for it. Engineers built smaller batteries because customers wanted thinner phones. Cameras went from terrible to studio-quality because billions of people wanted to share their lives in pictures. Apps for navigation, food delivery, and music streaming exist because we voted with our taps and downloads.

That is the two-way street. Technology shapes the way society works. Society — through its needs, laws, money, and choices — shapes the technology right back.

Picture a real example. Twenty years ago, almost nobody worried about how much screen time a kid got, because nobody had a smartphone. Once everyone had one, schools, parents, and even doctors started having opinions. Some of those opinions turned into laws — like rules about phones in classrooms or digital privacy for kids under 13. Now phone makers and app companies have to design around those laws.

Tech pushed us. We pushed back. Tech adjusted. Round and round.

📘 Vocabulary

Technology — any tool, system, or method people invent to solve a problem or make life easier. (A pencil, a phone, and a vaccine are all technology.)

Society — a group of people living together with shared rules, customs, and ways of life.

Two-way relationship — when two things change each other in both directions. Tech changes society, and society changes tech.

💭 Think About It

Three years ago, "voice assistant" mostly meant Siri or Alexa. Today, you can ask an AI to write you a poem, summarize a video, or explain a math problem. Did people ask for that? Or did the technology arrive first, and we are still deciding how to handle it?

Hold that question. We will keep coming back to it all week.

02
Section 2 · Big Inventions, Big Changes

Five inventions that quietly rewrote daily life

If you want to see the two-way street in action, look back. Every few generations, an invention shows up that doesn't just change one thing — it rewires how people live, work, learn, and talk to each other.

Here are five of the loudest examples in human history. Watch how each one solved a problem society had — and then created brand-new problems society had to solve.

1
The printing press (1440s)
Before Gutenberg's press, books were copied by hand — one at a time, by candle, for months. Only the very rich and a few scholars owned them. The printing press cranked out hundreds of copies in the time it took a monk to finish one. Suddenly regular people could read. Schools grew. Newspapers were born. Whole new ideas spread across continents in weeks instead of decades.
2
The telephone (1876)
Before the telephone, "talking to someone far away" meant writing a letter and waiting weeks. Alexander Graham Bell's invention let your voice jump across cities in seconds. Friendships, businesses, and even emergencies (911 came from telephones!) reshaped around being able to call someone right now.
3
The radio (early 1900s)
Radio was the first technology that could deliver one person's voice into millions of homes at the same time. Families gathered in the evening to hear news, music, and stories. For the first time, an entire country could listen to the same announcement on the same night. (Sound familiar? That's basically what streaming is doing today, just faster.)
4
The internet (1970s–80s)
Originally built so a few research labs could share files, the internet exploded into something nobody planned: a global library, marketplace, classroom, and town square — all at once. It rewrote how people shop, learn, find jobs, watch shows, and even fall in love. It also brought new questions about privacy, lies online, and who controls information.
5
The smartphone (2007 — iPhone)
When the iPhone launched, calling it "a phone" was almost a joke. It was a camera, a map, a music player, a calculator, a flashlight, a notebook, a wallet, and a connection to the entire internet — in your pocket. Within ten years, more than half the planet carried one. Whole industries — taxis, music stores, paper maps — were reshaped overnight.
6
Modern AI assistants (2020s)
In just the past few years, AI tools that can write, draw, code, and answer questions in plain English went from research projects to apps your classmates use during homework. Schools, employers, and lawmakers are still racing to figure out the right rules — proof that society is in the middle of shaping this one right now, while you watch.

Notice the pattern? Each invention started by solving a problem (sharing knowledge, reaching far away, getting around faster) — and each one ended up changing things nobody predicted. The printing press wasn't trying to spark religious reform or modern democracy, but it did. The smartphone wasn't trying to put taxi drivers out of work, but it did.

That's the two-way street — and the cool part is, it has never stopped. Whatever the next big invention is, it's being built right now, probably by people not much older than you.

    💡 Did You Know?

    When the telephone was first demonstrated, some newspapers called it "a useless toy." When the early internet was shown to business leaders in the 1980s, many of them said no one would ever shop online. Today, both predictions look hilariously wrong.

    It's almost a rule: people underestimate how much a new technology will change society — until it has already happened.

    03
    Section 3 · Tech & Work

    What we automate, what we invent

    Whenever a powerful new technology shows up, the same nervous question shows up with it: "Is this going to take people's jobs?"

    The honest answer is: some, yes — and it also creates new ones. Both of those things have happened with every wave of invention in history.

    The fancy word for "letting a machine do the boring repetitive work" is automation. Automation isn't new. A washing machine automated laundry. An ATM automated walking up to a bank teller for cash. Self-checkout automated part of the grocery clerk's job. Each of those changed work — but life kept going.

    ⚙️ Already automated
    Jobs machines now help with
    • Factory robots — welding, painting, assembling cars
    • ATMs — cash withdrawals and deposits
    • Self-checkout lanes at the grocery store
    • Automatic phone systems ("press 1 for…")
    • Email spam filters that catch junk before you see it
    • GPS systems that replaced paper maps
    🌱 Brand-new jobs
    Careers that didn't exist before
    • App developer — designs and builds mobile apps
    • Cybersecurity analyst — defends networks from hackers
    • Data scientist — finds patterns in huge data sets
    • AI prompt engineer — crafts instructions for AI tools
    • Social media manager — runs a brand's online voice
    • Cloud architect — designs giant online systems

    Most of the jobs in that right-hand column did not exist when your parents were your age. The kids who are 11 today will probably end up in jobs that don't have names yet. That's not scary — it's actually one of the best parts of living in a fast-moving world.

    Here's the trade-off, though: when automation arrives suddenly, the people whose jobs are replaced don't always get a smooth path to the new ones. A factory worker can't become a data scientist overnight. Society's job — through schools, training programs, and laws — is to help people make that jump. Which is one of the biggest reasons your generation is learning computer science in 6th grade.

    🌎 Real-World Connection

    In 1900, almost half of Americans worked on farms. Today, fewer than 2% do — but the country produces more food than ever, because tractors, irrigation, and modern seeds did the heavy lifting. The other 98% didn't disappear. They became teachers, engineers, nurses, programmers, mechanics, designers — jobs that didn't exist (or barely existed) on those old farms.

    That's the long story of automation: not "machines replace people," but "machines free people up to do new things."

    04
    Section 4 · Tech & Access

    Who gets to use the tools — and who gets left out

    Here's a thing nobody likes to admit: a brand-new technology only matters if you can actually use it. And there are millions of people in the world — including in your own state — for whom the latest tech is locked behind a wall.

    That wall has two big sides: accessibility and the digital divide.

    Accessibility means: the technology works for people with different bodies, brains, eyes, ears, and hands. A website with tiny gray-on-white text is technically public, but a person with low vision can't read it. A video with no captions is invisible to a deaf student. A game that requires fast clicks excludes someone with limited hand mobility.

    Good news: tech designers have invented amazing accessibility tools. The trick is making sure they're built into every product, not bolted on as an afterthought.

    📖 Screen Readers
    Software that reads what's on the screen out loud — essential for people who are blind or have low vision.
    🎙️ Voice Control
    Lets users open apps, type messages, and navigate just by speaking — huge help for people who can't use a mouse or keyboard.
    📺 Captions & Subtitles
    Text version of audio and dialogue. Makes content available to deaf and hard-of-hearing users — and helps everyone in noisy spaces.
    🔍 Text Size & Contrast
    Letting users zoom in, switch to high-contrast mode, or pick a dyslexia-friendly font keeps reading possible for many more eyes.

    Now the second wall: the digital divide. That's the gap between people who have fast, reliable internet and modern devices — and people who don't.

    You might think "everyone has the internet now." But in the United States alone, millions of homes — especially in rural areas and low-income neighborhoods — still don't have high-speed broadband. Some kids have to do homework in a fast-food parking lot to get Wi-Fi. Some seniors can't book doctor appointments online because the nearest reliable connection is miles away. During the pandemic, students without home internet fell behind students who had it, sometimes for entire school years.

    The digital divide isn't just a tech problem. It's a fairness problem. If your future opportunities — jobs, school, doctors, voting — all happen online, then access to the internet starts to look a lot like access to opportunity itself.

    💭 Think About It

    Imagine your school assigned an essay tonight that requires watching a 20-minute video and submitting through Canvas by 8 a.m. Now imagine your home internet drops every 10 minutes, or your only device is a phone with a cracked screen.

    How would your night go? How fair would it be to grade your essay against a classmate with fiber internet and a brand-new laptop?

    05
    Section 5 · The Responsibility Question

    Power tools come with rules — even invisible ones

    Here's something a lot of people don't say out loud: technology is morally neutral. A drill can build a hospital or break a wall. A camera can document the truth or invade someone's privacy. A piece of code can teach kids to read or steal their data.

    Tech is not the villain. Tech is not the hero. The choices people make with tech — that's where the story gets interesting. The study of those choices — what is fair, what is right, what hurts people, what helps them — is called ethics.

    Three big ethics questions show up in almost every tech debate today.

    🔒 Privacy
    Who gets to know things about you? Every app you open quietly collects information — your location, what you click, who your friends are. Privacy is your right to control how that information is used. A smart digital citizen reads permissions, picks strong settings, and asks before sharing pictures of friends.
    ♻️ Sustainability
    What is the cost to the planet? Phones, laptops, and gaming consoles are made of metals dug out of the earth. When we toss them, the leftover plastic and chemicals become e-waste. Data centers — the warehouses behind every app you use — burn massive amounts of electricity. Sustainability means designing tech (and using it) with the planet in mind.
    🤝 Digital Citizenship
    How do you treat people online? Every comment, like, share, and DM is a tiny choice. A good digital citizen is honest, kind, gives credit, doesn't pile on, doesn't share private things without asking, and pushes back (politely) when they see something that's not okay.

    None of those three questions has a perfect answer. Smart adults disagree about them all the time. The point isn't to memorize a "right answer" — it's to notice that the question is there. The world's biggest tech mistakes have almost always come from people who didn't pause long enough to ask.

    You'll notice that all three connect back to that two-way street from Section 1. Privacy laws (society) push tech companies to change their data practices (technology). Customer pressure for greener phones (society) pushes manufacturers to use recycled materials (technology). Schools teaching digital citizenship (society) shape how the next generation behaves online (technology). The loop never stops.

    💡 Did You Know?

    The world produces over 50 million tons of e-waste every year — old phones, broken laptops, dead batteries, cables. That's heavier than every commercial airliner in the world, combined. Less than a quarter of it gets recycled properly. The rest leaks toxic metals into landfills.

    Every time you keep a working device a year longer, or recycle one properly, you're voting on the kind of future tech industry you want.

    ✅ Check for Understanding

    Pause and try: Pick one app on your phone (or one website you use a lot). Name one privacy choice, one sustainability impact, and one digital-citizenship habit connected to it. There are no wrong answers — just notice what's actually there.

    📚 Key Vocabulary — Week 22

    Technology
    Any tool, system, or method invented to solve a problem or make a task easier — from pencils to phones to vaccines.
    Society
    A group of people living together with shared rules, customs, languages, and ways of life.
    Ethics
    Thinking carefully about what is right and fair — especially when tech choices affect other people.
    Automation
    Using machines or software to do work that humans used to do by hand, like factory robots or self-checkout lanes.
    Accessibility
    Designing technology so people with different abilities — vision, hearing, motor skills, learning — can all use it.
    Digital divide
    The gap between people who have fast internet and modern devices and people who don't — and the unfair effects that gap creates.
    Privacy
    Your right to control what personal information is collected about you, and how it's shared or used.
    Sustainability
    Making and using technology in ways that don't wreck the environment — think recycling, energy use, and fewer toxic materials.

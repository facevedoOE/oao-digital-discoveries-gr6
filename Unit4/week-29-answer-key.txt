Q1. Which BEST describes a digital model?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) An exact, perfect copy of something real
     ❌ A model is SIMPLIFIED, not perfect — that's the whole
        point. A perfect copy would BE the real thing.
  B) A simplified version of something real, built from numbers
     and rules so we can study or predict it                    ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Exactly. A digital model uses numbers and rules to make
        a usable, simplified stand-in for the real thing.
        (SC.6.PE.3.1)
  C) Any photograph saved on a computer
     ❌ A photo is static. A model can be RUN, changed, and used
        to predict behavior.
  D) Hardware shaped like the thing it represents
     ❌ Digital models are made of code, not physical hardware.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q2. You're modeling how long a snowman lasts on a sunny day.
    Which is the BEST example of a VARIABLE in this model?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) The freezing point of water (0°C)
     ❌ A constant — it doesn't change. A variable is a value
        that CAN change.
  B) Outside temperature (changes hour by hour, day by day)     ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Outside temperature changes — that's exactly what a
        variable is. The model checks the variable to predict
        melting. (SC.6.PE.3.1)
  C) The number of pi (π)
     ❌ π is a mathematical constant — never changes.
  D) The chemical formula for water (H₂O)
     ❌ A fixed fact, not a value that changes during a sim.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q3. Why do engineers run simulations of new airplane designs
    BEFORE building real planes?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Building real planes is illegal until simulation is done
     ❌ Not true — engineers DO build real planes. Simulations
        come first to reduce cost and risk.
  B) Simulations let engineers test many designs cheaply and
     safely, without risking real planes, time, or lives        ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Right — the big value is the safe, cheap "what if?"
        Engineers can try many designs before committing real
        money or lives. (SC.6.ET.1.1)
  C) Simulations always predict the future perfectly
     ❌ Simulations are powerful, never perfect. They help us
        prepare, not predict everything.
  D) Real planes don't work as well as simulated ones
     ❌ Real planes are the goal — simulation is a step toward
        them, not a replacement.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q4. Which is an example of HAPTIC FEEDBACK?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) A bright color on a screen
     ❌ Visual feedback. Haptic = something you FEEL.
  B) A loud sound from speakers
     ❌ Auditory feedback. Haptic = touch / feel.
  C) Your phone vibrating to alert you to a new message         ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ A vibration is haptic feedback — information you feel
        through your sense of touch. (SC.6.ET.1.1)
  D) A spoken voice from a smart speaker
     ❌ Auditory output, not haptic.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q5. Modeling city traffic. Which detail is BEST to abstract
    away (leave out)?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Cars per minute entering each highway
     ❌ One of the most important variables for traffic — never
        abstract away.
  B) Average speed limit on each road
     ❌ Directly affects how fast traffic moves. Keep it.
  C) The exact paint color of each car                          ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Paint color doesn't change how traffic flows — that's
        exactly the kind of detail a good model abstracts away.
        (SC.6.PE.3.1)
  D) Location of major intersections
     ❌ Intersections are where jams form — keep this.

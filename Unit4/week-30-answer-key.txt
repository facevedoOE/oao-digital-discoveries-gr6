Q1. Which of the following lists the five steps of the
    ENGINEERING DESIGN PROCESS in the correct order?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Build a prototype → Identify the need → Research → Improve
     → Develop solutions
     ❌ You can't build a prototype before you know what problem
        you're solving. The process always starts by identifying
        the need.
  B) Identify the need → Research → Develop solutions →
     Build a prototype and test → Gather feedback and improve   ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ The five steps are: identify the need → research →
        develop solutions → build a prototype and test → gather
        feedback and improve. The cycle then loops back.
        (SC.6.PE.3.2)
  C) Research → Identify the need → Test → Develop solutions →
     Improve
     ❌ Research can't come before you know what to research.
        You have to identify the need first.
  D) Improve → Test → Develop → Research → Identify the need
     ❌ That's the order reversed. The cycle starts with
        identifying the need; you can't improve a design before
        it exists.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q2. A class is designing a robot that MUST reach the end of a
    maze in under 90 seconds and MUST be built using only the
    parts in the school's robotics kit. Which statement is BEST?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Both rules are criteria — they describe what success looks
     like.
     ❌ Not quite. Criteria describe what success looks like;
        constraints are limits you can't cross. The parts-kit
        rule is a limit, not a goal.
  B) The 90-second time limit is a criterion (success goal);
     the parts-kit rule is a constraint (a limit).             ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ "Reach the end in under 90 seconds" is the success goal
        — that's a criterion. "Use only the school's robotics
        kit" is a hard limit you can't cross — that's a
        constraint. (SC.6.PE.3.2)
  C) Both rules are constraints — they're both limits.
     ❌ The 90-second rule is the goal, not a limit. Goals are
        criteria; limits are constraints. The two rules play
        different roles.
  D) The parts-kit rule is a criterion, and the time limit is
     a constraint.
     ❌ Reversed. The 90-second rule defines success
        (criterion); the parts-kit rule limits how you may
        build (constraint).

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q3. Which statement BEST describes ITERATION in the engineering
    design process?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Doing the design once and never changing it.
     ❌ Almost no real design is finished on the first try.
        Doing it once is the opposite of iteration.
  B) Starting over from scratch every time, with no memory of
     past attempts.
     ❌ Iteration carries lessons forward — that's the whole
        point. Starting over with no memory wastes the feedback
        you collected.
  C) Going around the design cycle again, using what you
     learned last time to make the next version better.        ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Iteration means looping the cycle again with what you
        learned last time. Each iteration should make the design
        better. (SC.6.PE.3.3)
  D) A sign of failure that good engineers avoid.
     ❌ Iteration is the opposite of failure — it's how
        excellence is actually built. Real engineers iterate on
        purpose.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q4. Your team has a working video-game prototype. The team has
    played it and it runs fine, but no one outside the team has
    ever tried it. What is the BEST next step in the design
    process?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Declare the prototype final — if the team likes it, it's
     ready.
     ❌ The team built it — of course they like it. The point
        of testing is to expose the design to people who didn't
        build it.
  B) Add 30 more features before showing anyone the game.
     ❌ Adding features without testing what you have just
        buries the bugs deeper. Test first, then improve.
  C) Get external testers to play it, gather feedback, and
     use that feedback to improve the design.                  ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Step 4–5 of the cycle: get external testers, gather
        feedback, and improve. A working prototype is exactly
        when external testing is most valuable. (SC.6.PE.3.3)
  D) Throw the prototype away and start a different game.
     ❌ A working prototype is a victory — don't throw it out
        before you've even tested it. Iterate on what you have
        first.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q5. A teammate says: "Our design works perfectly on the first
    try. We don't need to test it or change it — it's already
    finished." Which response is BEST, based on the engineering
    design process?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Agree — if it works, it's done.
     ❌ "Works for us" almost never means "works for everyone."
        The team built it, so they know how to use it. Real
        users will surprise you.
  B) Even a "finished" design usually needs more testing and
     iteration. Getting outside feedback almost always reveals
     problems we can't see ourselves.                          ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ The fifth step of the cycle exists precisely because
        designs that look "finished" almost always have problems
        the designers can't see. Iteration is a virtue, not a
        sign of failure. (SC.6.PE.3.3)
  C) Agree, but only if no one has complained yet.
     ❌ "No one has complained" usually just means no one has
        tested it. Silence isn't proof of success.
  D) Disagree, and throw the design out completely just to be
     safe.
     ❌ Trashing the whole design without testing is just as
        wasteful as shipping it without testing. The right move
        is to gather feedback and iterate.

Q1. Which BEST describes machine learning?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) A programmer types in every rule the software needs to
     follow, by hand
     ❌ That describes TRADITIONAL programming, not machine
        learning. Machine learning is special because the rules
        come from PATTERNS IN DATA, not from a programmer.
  B) Software that improves at a task by being shown lots of
     examples (training data) instead of being given hand-
     written rules                                              ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Exactly. Machine learning gets better by spotting
        patterns in examples — that's what makes it different
        from traditional programming. (SC.6.ET.2.1)
  C) A robot that physically learns by walking around a
     classroom
     ❌ Machine learning isn't about a physical robot — it's
        about how the software inside is built.
  D) Any program that runs on a fast computer
     ❌ Speed alone doesn't make a program AI. The defining
        trait of machine learning is that it adjusts itself
        based on examples.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q2. Which is BEST described as LEARNING-BASED software (not
    rules-based)?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) A microwave running for 30 seconds when you press "30 sec"
     ❌ Rules-based — fixed countdown, no learning.
  B) A traffic light cycling green-yellow-red on a fixed timer
     ❌ Rules-based — fixed schedule, no learning from real
        traffic.
  C) A streaming app that recommends your next show based on
     what you and people like you have watched                  ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ A recommender uses past behavior (yours and millions of
        others') to predict what you'll like next. Classic
        learning-based AI. (SC.6.ET.2.1)
  D) A calculator computing 7 × 8
     ❌ Rules-based — 7 × 8 always equals 56. The calculator
        doesn't get better with use.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q3. Which everyday tool is the BEST example of GENERATIVE AI?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) A chatbot that writes a brand-new poem about your dog when
     you ask it to                                              ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Generative AI creates new text, images, audio, or video
        by predicting what should come next based on training
        data. A chatbot writing a fresh poem fits perfectly.
        (SC.6.ET.2.1)
  B) A pocket calculator
     ❌ Rules-based math, not generation. It computes a known
        answer rather than creating something new.
  C) A digital clock
     ❌ Just a counter — nothing learned, nothing generated.
  D) A flashlight app
     ❌ Rules-based: tap = on, tap = off. No AI involved.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q4. An AI hiring tool consistently rejects qualified women
    applicants because it was trained mostly on past hires
    (mostly men). What is this — and what's the BEST first step?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) The AI is being "objective" — there's nothing to fix
     ❌ An AI that copies an unfair pattern is NOT objective —
        it's biased. "Objective" describes the math, not the
        outcome.
  B) Bias from biased training data — identify it, retrain on
     better data, add human review                              ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Textbook AI ethics problem. The model learned the
        unfairness in the data. Fix the data, add a human in
        the loop. (SC.6.ET.2.2)
  C) A privacy problem — the AI shouldn't see résumés
     ❌ Privacy is a real AI concern, but the issue here is
        unfair decisions caused by biased data — that's bias.
  D) An ownership problem
     ❌ Ownership is about who controls a creation. This
        problem is about FAIRNESS in evaluation.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q5. Your friend asks at 11pm: "Can I feed your essay into
    ChatGPT and have it write mine in the same style?" Using
    SOCRATIC / VIRTUE-ETHICS reasoning (especially PRUDENCE),
    what's the BEST response?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Send the essay without comment
     ❌ Plausible deniability isn't a virtue. Prudence asks you
        to think past the next moment.
  B) Yell at them and refuse to talk to them again
     ❌ Prudence and friendship aren't opposites. You can
        decline AND be kind.
  C) Decline kindly, name the integrity issue, and offer to
     help them outline their own essay even though it's late    ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Prudence + justice + friendship working together. You
        name the issue, decline the shortcut, AND offer real
        help. Exactly the kind of choice the cardinal virtues
        point toward. (SC.6.ET.2.2)
  D) Use ChatGPT to write your friend's essay for them yourself
     ❌ That just transfers the dishonesty from your friend to
        you. Prudence rules it out.

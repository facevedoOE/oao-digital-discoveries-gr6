The Engineering Design Process

No great invention was ever finished on the first try. Bicycles, airplanes, video games, smartphones — every one of them was rebuilt over and over by people willing to throw away their first idea and try again. This week, you'll meet the cycle every great inventor follows, and you'll see why iteration is a virtue, not a sign of failure.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

1
Name and order the five steps of the engineering design process.
2
Distinguish criteria from constraints when evaluating a design — what success looks like vs. what limits you.
3
Compare two or more design ideas using trade-offs and feedback to pick the best path forward.
4
Explain why iteration is a virtue — and why throwing work away is sometimes the most professional thing you can do.
01
Section 1 · Why Every Great Thing Was Rebuilt

Nothing useful is finished on the first try

The first bicycle had no pedals. You pushed it along the ground with your feet like a scooter. The first airplane to fly — the Wright brothers' Flyer — flew for twelve seconds before crashing into a sand dune. The first iPhone couldn't copy and paste. The first version of the video game Minecraft was so simple it didn't even have crafting in it.

Every one of those things was eventually rebuilt — sometimes dozens of times, sometimes thousands. The thing you use today is the great-great-grandchild of the thing the inventor first tried. The reason it works so well isn't because the inventor was a genius on day one. It's because the inventor was willing to try, fail, look honestly at the failure, and then build again.

Engineers and computer scientists give this cycle a name: the engineering design process. It has five steps, and they go around in a loop, not a straight line. You don't reach the end and stop. You go around, and around, and around, getting closer each time.

    📘 Vocabulary

    Engineering design process — the five-step cycle (identify, research, develop, test, improve) that engineers, designers, and programmers use to turn an idea into a working solution.

    Iteration — going around the cycle again with what you learned last time. Each loop is one iteration.

    02
    Section 2 · Steps 1–2 · Identify the Need + Research

    Finding the right problem is half the work

    Beginners think the hardest part of building something is the building. Veterans know the hardest part is figuring out what to build in the first place. The first two steps of the design process exist to slow you down and make you ask: do I even understand the problem yet?

    Step 1 — Identify the need or problem. Who is this for? What do they actually struggle with? Engineers call this the user. If you skip this step, you build something nobody wants — a beautiful solution to a problem that doesn't exist.

    Step 2 — Research and gather information. Has anyone solved this before? What did they try? What worked, and what didn't? Most "new" inventions are clever combinations of older ones. The fastest path forward is often to learn what already exists, then figure out where it falls short.

    🌎 Real-World Connection

    When the team behind the Roomba was trying to invent a robot vacuum, their first idea was a robot that looked like a tiny butler. They built one. Nobody wanted it. They went back to step 1 and asked: "What do people actually hate about cleaning?" The answer was vacuuming under furniture. So they built a flat disc that could fit under a couch. That second design — born from re-doing the research — is the one that became famous.

    03
    Section 3 · Step 3 · Develop & Compare Solutions

    Criteria, constraints, and the art of trade-offs

    Once you understand the problem, your job is to come up with more than one possible solution and compare them side-by-side. Designers almost never pick the first idea — they sketch three or four and then ask: which one is best? But "best" has to be measured against something. That's where two key words come in.

    Criteria are what success looks like — the goals your design must hit. ("It must be fun to play." "It must take less than 30 seconds to use." "It must be readable from across a room.") Criteria are how you decide whether a solution is good.

    Constraints are the limits you can't cross — budget, time, materials, weight, the size of the robotics-kit motor, the rules of the contest. Constraints aren't goals — they're walls. A great design hits the criteria without breaking through the constraints.

    🎯 Criteria
    What success looks like
    • "The drone must hover for at least 5 minutes."
    • "The app must work for left-handed users."
    • "The chair must be comfortable for 1 hour."
    • "The robot must beat the maze in under 90 seconds."
    🚧 Constraint
    A limit you can't cross
    • "Total cost must stay under $50."
    • "You only have until Friday."
    • "It must fit inside a backpack."
    • "You can only use parts from the school's robotics kit."

    When two ideas both hit the criteria, you choose between them with a trade-off. A trade-off is the honest acknowledgment that you can't have everything. A faster drone uses more battery. A simpler app loses a feature. A cheaper robot is slower. Engineering is full of trade-offs, and choosing wisely is the whole skill.

    💡 Did You Know?

    Most professional designers use digital tools to compare ideas before they build anything. CAD software lets engineers spin a 3-D model around to spot problems. Apps like Figma let UI designers click through three different layouts in five minutes. Spreadsheets let teams score each idea against the criteria. The faster you can compare designs, the better the design you ship.

    04
    Section 4 · Steps 4–5 · Test, Get Feedback, Improve

    Build a prototype, break it, and rebuild it better

    Now comes the part where you actually build something. But here's the surprise: the first thing you build is not supposed to be the final version. It's a prototype — a quick, rough draft made on purpose so you can find what's wrong with it. The whole point of a prototype is to fail safely, on a small scale, before failing big.

    Once you have a prototype, you test it. You don't test it alone in your head, either — you put it in front of someone else and watch what happens. The information you collect when other people use your design is called feedback. Feedback is sometimes painful. Someone will say your game is confusing. Someone will say your robot tipped over. Someone will say your sentence doesn't make sense. Listen anyway.

    Then comes step 5: improve. You take the feedback, decide what to keep and what to change, and you make a new version. That's revision — and it's also the moment when the cycle quietly loops back to step 1 again. Every improvement reveals a new problem. So you go around again. And again.

    1
    Identify the need
    Find the real user. State the real problem. Don't skip ahead.
    2
    Research
    Find out what people have already tried. Learn from the work that came before yours.
    3
    Develop solutions
    Sketch more than one idea. Compare them against your criteria and within your constraints. Choose with eyes open.
    4
    Build a prototype and test it
    Make a rough version on purpose. Put it in front of real users. Collect feedback honestly.
    5
    Gather feedback and improve
    Revise based on what you learned. Then go around the cycle again. The loop is the whole point.
    ✅ Check for Understanding

    A team is designing a paper-airplane that must fly at least 6 meters and must be made from one sheet of letter paper. Which of those is a criterion, and which is a constraint? (Hint: one defines success, the other limits how you achieve it.)

    05
    Section 5 · Iteration as a Classical Virtue

    Why the willingness to throw work away is a kind of courage

    The Greeks and Romans had a word for the steady, repeated effort it takes to reach excellence: they called it part of fortitudo — what we now translate as fortitude. Fortitude isn't only courage in a single dramatic moment. It's the patience to keep working when the first version, the second version, and the third version are all wrong, and you still believe the right version is reachable.

    The medieval craftsmen who built cathedrals went through dozens of design revisions over decades. The painters who came after them did the same — Leonardo da Vinci's notebooks are full of sketches of the same idea drawn ten different ways, until the tenth one was right. Today's engineers, programmers, and designers stand inside that long tradition. The tools are different. The discipline is the same.

    What classical thinkers understood — and what every great engineer rediscovers — is that iteration is not failure. Iteration is how excellence is actually made. The first try almost never produces something worth keeping. The willingness to look at your own first try and say "this isn't good enough yet" — and then start again without losing heart — is one of the most important habits a young scholar can build.

    💭 Think About It

    The classical idea of craftsmanship says that the goal of any good work — a sculpture, a house, an essay, a piece of code — is not to be finished quickly, but to be made well. Made-well takes iteration. When you revise your writing, when you rebuild your robotics design, when you debug your code, you're practicing one of the oldest virtues in Western education: the patient pursuit of excellence.

    📚 Key Vocabulary — Week 30

    Engineering design process
    The five-step cycle (identify, research, develop, test, improve) used to turn an idea into a working solution.
    Criteria
    The goals a design must hit — what success looks like for the user.
    Constraint
    A limit you can't cross — budget, time, materials, size, or the rules of the project.
    Prototype
    A rough first version of a design, built on purpose so you can find what's wrong with it before committing to a final.
    Iteration
    Going around the design cycle again, using what you learned last time to make the next version better.
    Feedback
    Information you gather from real users about how your design actually performs.
    Revision
    A new version of a design produced by acting on feedback — the practical result of step 5.
    Trade-off
    An honest exchange where gaining one quality (speed, cost, simplicity) means giving up another. Engineering choices are full of them.
    🪞 Weekly Reflection
    Pick one thing you've made — and walk it through the cycle.

    Choose one thing you've actually built or made: a school project, a piece of art, some code, a story you wrote, even a recipe you cooked. Then answer in 4–6 sentences:

    • What was the need or problem the thing was trying to solve?
    • Name one criterion you used to judge whether it was good.
    • Name one constraint you had to design within (time, materials, your own skill, etc.).
    • What changed between your first version and your final version? In other words, what did iteration teach you about your own work?

Section 1 · The Big Arc

From numbers in a box to the four cardinal virtues

It might not be obvious yet, but Unit 4 has been telling one story. Let's walk through it backwards so the shape stands out.

You ended at ethics — questions about what's right and prudent when using AI. You can only ask those questions because, the week before, you learned what AI is: software that learns from data and finds patterns. And you can only see why AI is special once you've contrasted it with a human partner — what humans bring (judgment, empathy, ethical reasoning) and what machines bring (speed, precision, tireless repetition). Two weeks before that, you learned the engineering design process — how iteration turns rough ideas into polished work. And under all of that sits modeling — the original move of pulling a piece of the world into a computer as numbers and rules.

So the unit's arc, in plain English: You can only build something well if you can model it. You can only refine it if you iterate. You can only deploy it wisely if you understand who should do what. And you can only judge it justly if you reason about it as a virtuous person would.

Section 3 · Connecting Models + AI

Why "learning-based" is just a smarter kind of model

Here's a connection that becomes obvious once you've seen both pieces: a learning-based AI is just a model whose conditions were not handwritten.

In Week 29, you ran the Pond Simulator. The IF/THEN rules — "if temperature is above 32°C, fish stress increases" — were written by a human. That's a rules-based model.

In Week 32, you saw learning-based software like Spotify recommendations or face recognition. Those are also models — they have variables (your listening history; the pixels of a face) and they produce outputs (a song; a name). But the conditions weren't typed in by an engineer. The system learned them by sifting through huge amounts of training data. That's the only difference.

Why does this matter? Because every concern you learned about in Week 32 — bias, privacy, ownership — comes from where the conditions came from. Bias slips in when training data isn't fair. Privacy concerns come from training data being personal. Ownership questions come from training data being someone else's work. The model is only as just as the data that taught it. A computer is only as fair as the world that taught it to count.

💭 Think About It

The classical thinkers held that wisdom requires both knowing the truth and knowing your own ignorance. When we use AI we are doing both at once: trusting that the model has learned something true, and remaining alert to the gap between what it knows and what it pretends to know. That second virtue — humility before the limits of any tool — is what keeps a smart user from becoming a careless one.

04
Section 4 · The Ethical Thread

Why this whole unit centers on virtue

You may have noticed something unusual about Unit 4. We didn't just teach you how to build technology. We kept asking whether you should — and how you should. That was on purpose.

The four cardinal virtues from the classical tradition — prudence (wise foresight), justice (giving each person their due), fortitude (perseverance through difficulty), and temperance (moderation in pleasure and use) — were never just about ancient life. They map onto modern decisions about AI startlingly well.

⚖️ Justice in AI
Bias, fairness, ownership
  • Asking whether training data was collected fairly.
  • Crediting human creators when their work shaped a tool.
  • Refusing to deploy a model that systematically harms a group.
🦉 Prudence in AI
Wise foresight
  • Knowing when to delegate to AI and when to stay hands-on.
  • Reading the privacy settings before pressing "agree."
  • Asking "what could go wrong?" before celebrating a launch.
💪 Fortitude in AI
Doing the hard work yourself
  • Writing your own essay even when AI offers a shortcut.
  • Learning a skill instead of always outsourcing it.
  • Iterating on a real prototype instead of giving up.
🌿 Temperance in AI
Moderation, not addiction
  • Putting the phone down when the algorithm wants you scrolling.
  • Using generative tools to assist, not replace, your thinking.
  • Choosing depth of attention over endless feed.

🌎 Real-World Connection

Lawyers, scientists, and journalists do exactly this move every day. They take a position — "this medicine works," "this contract was breached," "this election was clean" — and then they build a chain of evidence under it. The skill they're really exercising isn't writing or speaking. It's recognizing which evidence is on point and which is a distraction. That's the same skill you just practiced.

📚 Key Vocabulary — Week 33

Synthesis
Bringing several ideas together to form a single, bigger picture.
Position
The claim you are making — what you believe is true and want others to consider.
Evidence
A fact, example, or quote that backs up your position.
Counterargument
A reason a thoughtful person might disagree with your position — worth knowing so you can answer it.
Infographic
A picture-and-text design that makes information clear at a glance.
Presentation
A short series of slides or a video that walks an audience through your argument.
Virtue
A trained habit of doing the right thing well — like prudence, justice, fortitude, or temperance.
Perspective
The viewpoint from which a person sees an issue, shaped by their experience and reasoning.

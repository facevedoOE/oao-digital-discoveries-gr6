UNIT 4 • WEEK 32 • READING

AI Fundamentals & Ethics

Artificial intelligence already writes essays, recommends songs, recognizes your face, and answers your questions. But how does it actually work — and how should a thoughtful person decide when to use it? This week, you'll separate the magic from the math, and you'll borrow some help from the ancient Greeks to think clearly about what's just, prudent, and true.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

1
Explain what makes a piece of software "intelligent" — and why machine learning is different from traditional programming.
2
Distinguish rules-based software from learning-based software, and identify examples of each.
3
Identify ethical questions that AI raises — bias, privacy, ownership, and the future of work.
4
Apply the four cardinal virtues — prudence, justice, fortitude, and temperance — to real decisions about using AI.
01
Section 1 · What Makes AI "Intelligent"?

Learning from data — not just following orders

Imagine teaching a younger sibling to recognize cats. You don't hand them a 200-page manual titled "How to Identify a Cat." You show them cats. Lots of cats. Big cats, small cats, fluffy cats, hairless cats, photographs of cats, the cat next door. After a while, they just know. They don't memorize a list of rules — they pick up the pattern.

That, more or less, is how artificial intelligence learns too. The clever trick at the heart of modern AI is called machine learning: instead of a programmer writing every rule by hand, the computer is shown millions of examples — called training data — and it adjusts itself until it can spot the pattern on its own.

This is a real shift. A traditional program knows facts because a human typed them in. A machine-learning program recognizes patterns because it found them inside a mountain of data. Knowing facts and recognizing patterns are two very different skills — and AI is mostly about the second one.

📘 Vocabulary

Artificial intelligence (AI) — software that performs tasks people normally call "intelligent": recognizing patterns, generating language, making predictions, choosing what to recommend.

Machine learning — the most common kind of AI; software that gets better at a task by being shown lots of examples instead of being given hand-written rules.

Training data — the giant pile of examples used to teach a machine-learning model the pattern.

02
Section 2 · Rules-Based vs Learning-Based

Two very different ways to make a computer "smart"

Not all software is AI. Most isn't. The world is filled with rules-based software that follows a fixed list of IF/THEN instructions written by a human. Your microwave is rules-based. So is a calculator. So is a basic thermostat. They're useful, but they don't learn anything. They do exactly the same thing today as they did yesterday.

Learning-based software is different. It improves over time as more data flows in. The Spotify recommender that knows you like indie folk knows it because it watched what you played, what you skipped, and what people like you played next. It wasn't programmed to recommend that song — it learned.

Knowing which kind of software you're dealing with matters, because it tells you what to expect. A rules-based system is predictable: same input, same output. A learning-based system can be surprising — and sometimes wrong in ways even its creators didn't expect.

📐 Rules-Based
Fixed instructions written by a human
  • Calculator: 7 × 8 = 56, every time
  • Thermostat: IF temp < 70 THEN turn on heater
  • Microwave timer: count down 30 seconds
  • Traffic light cycle: green → yellow → red on a timer
🧠 Learning-Based
Patterns picked up from data
  • Spotify suggesting your next song
  • Email filter learning what spam looks like
  • Camera recognizing a friend's face
  • ChatGPT writing a poem about your dog

Section 3 · AI Tools You've Already Used

It's been in your pocket the whole time

You don't need to log into a fancy lab to meet AI. By lunchtime today you've probably interacted with half a dozen AI systems without thinking about it. Here are some of the big ones.

🎵 Recommendation Systems
YouTube's "up next." Netflix's "because you watched." Spotify's Discover Weekly. TikTok's For You feed. All are AI engines that compare your viewing history against patterns from millions of other users to predict what you'll want next.
💬 Chatbots & Generative AI
ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, image generators like DALL·E or Midjourney. These models read or saw enormous amounts of text and pictures, and now generate new text or images by predicting what should come next, one piece at a time.
📷 Image & Voice Recognition
Your phone's camera unlocking when it sees your face. Voice transcription on a video call. Auto-captioning on YouTube. Search-by-photo in Google. All trained on huge labeled datasets of images or audio.
⌨️ Autocomplete & Autocorrect
When your phone suggests the next word as you type, that's a mini language model running locally. Same idea as a chatbot, but predicting the next word rather than a whole essay.
💡 Did You Know?

The biggest modern AI models are trained on trillions of words of text — more than any single person could read in 100,000 lifetimes. That's why they can write so fluently. But it's also why they sometimes confidently make things up: they're predicting what sounds right, not checking what's true.

04
Section 4 · The Ethical Questions

Powerful tools come with hard questions

AI can do remarkable things. It can also cause real harm. Both can be true at the same time. As AI gets more powerful, the people who use it well will be the ones who can name the hard questions and think them through. Here are four of the biggest ones.

1
Bias — when training data is unfair, the AI learns the unfairness
An AI hiring tool was trained on a decade of past hires. Past hires skewed male — so the AI learned that "qualified" looked male and started rejecting women. The AI wasn't sexist on purpose; it just copied the pattern in the data. Garbage in, garbage out.
2
Privacy — your data is the fuel
Most AI tools learn from data, and a lot of that data is people. Voice assistants record voices. Chatbots remember conversations. Photo apps catalogue faces. Some of it makes the tool better. Some of it ends up sold to advertisers, or leaked, or used in ways the original people never agreed to.
3
Ownership — whose creativity is it, anyway?
When an image generator copies the style of a living artist who never gave permission, who owns the result? When a student uses ChatGPT to write a whole essay, whose work was it? These aren't trick questions — they're real, unsolved debates happening right now in courts and classrooms.
4
Jobs — what happens when machines do the work?
Some jobs change because of AI. Some disappear. Some new ones appear. History suggests the picture is mixed: machines have replaced human work many times before (think of the printing press, the cotton gin, the spreadsheet), and people adapted. But the transition is rarely smooth, and the question of whom AI helps and whom it hurts is one we have to keep asking.
🌎 Real-World Connection

In 2018, a major retailer scrapped an AI tool it had been building for years to screen résumés — because the model had taught itself to penalize résumés that contained the word "women's" (as in "women's chess club"). The engineers didn't program that in. The data did. Catching that bias was the work of careful, thoughtful humans — not the machine.

Section 5 · Thinking Like Socrates About AI

The four cardinal virtues meet the chatbot

How do you actually decide what's the right thing to do with a powerful new tool? You ask the questions Socrates asked his students 2,400 years ago: What is just? What is prudent? What is true? The classical thinkers grouped the deepest answers under four virtues — the four cardinal virtues. They still work, even when the question is "should I use ChatGPT to write this essay?"

🦉 Prudence
Wise foresight. Before you use an AI tool, ask: what could go wrong, and who would it hurt? Prudence isn't fear — it's the habit of thinking past the next click.
⚖️ Justice
Giving each person their due. If an AI is biased against some group, or copies an artist's style without consent, justice is the virtue that says "this is not fair, and I won't pretend it is."
💪 Fortitude
Courage to do the hard work. When AI offers you a shortcut around real learning — writing your essay, solving your math problem — fortitude is the virtue that says "I'll do it myself, even though it's harder."
🌿 Temperance
Moderation in use. A great tool can become a bad habit. Temperance asks: am I using AI to help me grow, or to avoid effort? Am I still capable of doing this on my own?
💭 Think About It

Socrates famously said the unexamined life is not worth living. The unexamined tool isn't either. Every AI you use is making thousands of small choices on your behalf — what song to play, what text to suggest, what face to recognize. The four cardinal virtues — prudence, justice, fortitude, and temperance — give you a checklist for examining those choices instead of letting them happen to you.

📚 Key Vocabulary — Week 32

Machine learning
A kind of software that improves at a task by being shown lots of examples, instead of being given hand-written rules.
Training data
The collection of examples used to teach a machine-learning model. The bigger and more representative the dataset, the better the model can perform.
Bias
An unfair pattern in an AI's behavior, usually caused by unfair patterns in its training data.
Recommendation system
An AI that predicts what content you'll like next, based on what you and people like you have liked before.
Generative AI
An AI that creates new text, images, audio, or video by predicting what should come next based on training data.
Ethics
The careful study of what's right and wrong, and how to act well — especially when the choice is hard.
Prudence
The cardinal virtue of wise foresight — thinking carefully about what may follow from a choice before making it.
Justice
The cardinal virtue of giving each person their due — fairness, especially when a system might treat people unequally.

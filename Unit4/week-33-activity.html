AI Perspectives Project

Take a position on AI's role in society. Build the case. Make it beautiful.

The Brief

Pick a question about AI's role in our world. Stake out a position on it. Then build a short, polished deliverable — an infographic, a 5–7 slide presentation, or a 90-second video — that walks a reader (who hasn't taken this course) from your question to your answer, with evidence along the way.

This isn't an essay assignment. It's a persuasion assignment. You'll be graded on whether your reader can clearly understand and evaluate your position — not on how many words you wrote.

Pick One Format
📊
Infographic
A single image (poster-size) that combines headlines, short paragraphs, and visual elements. Works in Canva, Google Slides as a single slide, or even hand-drawn and photographed.
🖼️
Slide Deck
5 to 7 slides in Google Slides, PowerPoint, or Keynote. Each slide has one clear idea. Total reading time: about 90 seconds.
🎬
Short Video
A 60–90 second narrated video. Could be a screen recording, slideshow with voiceover, or filmed clip. Phone audio is fine.
Suggested Topics (or pick your own)
Education
Should students be allowed to use AI tools (like ChatGPT) for school assignments? Where's the line between help and cheating?
Creativity
Is AI-generated art real art? Does it help or hurt human artists?
Privacy
Should we be worried about how much our voice assistants and apps know about us? What protections do we deserve?
Work
Will AI eliminate jobs, create new ones, or change what humans do at work? Which?
Justice
Should AI be allowed to make decisions in courts, hiring, or hospitals? Where must humans always stay in the loop?
Virtue
What would a truly prudent sixth grader's relationship with AI look like? Pick one of the four cardinal virtues and apply it.
Structure — 5 parts to include
1
Your question
A clear question your project will answer. Should be focused enough to argue in 90 seconds. Examples: "Should middle schoolers use AI for homework?" · "Is AI art a form of theft from human artists?"
2
Your position
A one-sentence claim that answers your question. Strong positions are specific and clear. Avoid mush like "it depends" — that's not a position, it's an avoidance.
3
Three pieces of evidence
Three concrete reasons your position is correct. Each should be a fact, an example, a real-world story, or a quote — not just a feeling. Use what you learned in Weeks 29–32 and from your own experience.
4
A counterargument — and your response
Name one reason a thoughtful person might disagree with you. Then explain why your position still holds. This is the move that separates a real argument from a rant.
5
A virtue connection
Tie your position to one of the four cardinal virtues — prudence, justice, fortitude, or temperance. How does choosing your position make a person more of one of those virtues?
Evaluation Rubric — 20 points total
Criterion Points
Clear, focused question and a specific position4
Three pieces of strong, on-point evidence6
A real counterargument and your response to it4
A virtue connection (prudence / justice / fortitude / temperance)3
Visual or audio polish — readable, not crowded, on-brand3

An "A" project (18+) makes a clear case a stranger could grasp in under two minutes. A "B" project (14–17) covers all five parts but rushes one or two. Anything below that is missing one of the five required pieces.

Polish Tips
  • One idea per slide / one section per panel of the infographic. Don't crowd.
  • Short headlines. Long thinking. Trust the reader to fill in some blanks.
  • If you cite a fact, cite where you got it. Even a parenthetical "(Week 32 reading)" is enough for this project.
  • Pick 2–3 brand colors and stick to them. The OAO palette works: navy blue, gold, teal, green, orange, purple.
  • Read it aloud. If you stumble, the audience will too.
How to Submit

Upload a single file (PDF, slide deck, image, or video) to your teacher's submission link. If your file is huge, share a Google Drive / OneDrive link instead.

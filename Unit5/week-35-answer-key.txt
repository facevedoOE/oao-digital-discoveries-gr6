Q1. Maria's job is to make sure that when you tap a button in
    an app, it feels right — that the layout, color, words, and
    flow guide you naturally and work for people with all kinds
    of abilities. Which CS career is Maria MOST likely in?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Cybersecurity analyst
     ❌ Cybersecurity analysts protect systems from attack.
        Maria's work is about how the app feels, not defense.
  B) UX designer                                                 ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ UX designers shape how a product feels and flows for
        the people using it — including making it accessible.
        (SC.6.ET.1.2)
  C) Data scientist
     ❌ Data scientists find patterns in big data sets. Maria's
        focus is the user experience, not the data.
  D) Network administrator
     ❌ Network admins keep computers and Wi-Fi connected. That's
        important work, but it's not about how apps feel to use.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q2. Daniela never finished college. Over four years she taught
    herself to code, built a public portfolio of real projects,
    and contributed to open-source software. Which statement
    about her chances of becoming a software engineer is MOST
    accurate?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Impossible — every software engineer must have a 4-year
     CS degree
     ❌ This is a common myth. Many software engineers came in
        through bootcamps, self-teaching, or apprenticeships.
  B) Self-teaching with a strong portfolio of real projects is
     a recognized path — many engineers came in this way        ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Practice on real problems is the common thread across
        every route into CS. A polished portfolio is real
        evidence of skill. (SC.6.ET.1.2)
  C) Only a bootcamp counts — anything else is just a hobby
     ❌ Bootcamps are one good path, but they're not the only
        one. Self-teaching, apprenticeship, and the degree path
        all work too.
  D) She'll have to start over with a brand-new 4-year CS
     degree before anyone will consider her
     ❌ She doesn't have to start over. Many companies hire
        based on demonstrated skill.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q3. You're a security engineer at a streaming company. You
    discover a critical bug that would let anyone steal user
    passwords. What is the MOST ethical FIRST move?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Quietly sell the bug to whoever offers the most money
     ❌ Selling a vulnerability to attackers harms real users.
        That's a clear ethical failure.
  B) Post the exact bug publicly on social media before the
     company can react
     ❌ Public disclosure before a fix is in place exposes
        users to attackers. Responsible disclosure goes to the
        company first.
  C) Report it to your security team and leadership
     immediately, then help patch it before users are harmed   ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Responsible disclosure — the ethical heart of
        cybersecurity. Report internally first, then help fix
        the bug before users are harmed. (SC.6.TI.2.1)
  D) Wait a year to see if anyone else finds it first
     ❌ Waiting just leaves users exposed. Speed protects
        people; hesitation harms them.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q4. A classmate posts a video of themselves tripping and
    spilling lunch everywhere. Comments are piling on. What is
    the MOST PRUDENT response, in the classical sense of
    prudence (wise foresight)?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Pile on with a clever joke — everyone else is doing it
     ❌ Piling on harms a real person for a small social
        reward. Prudence pauses before the joke.
  B) Screenshot and share to a group chat so more people see
     ❌ Amplifying someone's bad day is the opposite of
        prudence — it makes the harm bigger.
  C) Don't pile on. If it feels right, send the person a
     private message that's kind                                ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Prudence is the pause before publishing — and a small
        private kindness is justice in action. (SC.6.TI.1.1)
  D) Tell yourself it's "just the internet" and leave a sneaky
     negative emoji
     ❌ A "sneaky" negative reaction still adds harm. Prudence
        asks whether the click helps a real person — not
        whether it can be hidden.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q5. You feel like scrolling for another hour, but you're tired
    and have homework. You put the phone down. Which of the
    four cardinal virtues did you MOST clearly practice?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Prudence — wise foresight
     ❌ Prudence is the pause before deciding. The act of
        stopping when you've had enough is closer to temperance.
  B) Justice — giving people what is owed
     ❌ Justice is about how you treat others. This choice was
        about how you treat yourself.
  C) Temperance — self-mastery, knowing what is enough         ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Temperance is the virtue of knowing your limit and
        choosing it. Putting the phone down when enough is
        enough is temperance in action. (SC.6.TI.1.1)
  D) Fortitude — courage in hardship
     ❌ Fortitude does help, but the lead virtue here —
        knowing what is enough — is temperance.

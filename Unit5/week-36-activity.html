🏆 CAPSTONE GUIDE
Unit 5 · Week 36 · All Course Standards

Year-End Capstone Project

One project. Your choice of track. Everything you learned this year, locked into a single deliverable.

The Brief

Choose one of three tracks below. Plan it first. Build it. Polish it. Then submit a single deliverable that shows what you learned this year — and what kind of digital citizen you've become along the way.

This isn't a quiz. It's a piece of work you'll put your name on. Your teacher will assess the deliverable itself separately — this activity counts as complete when you've read the guide and made your plan.

Pick ONE Track
TRACK A
pulls from units 3 & 4
🧪 Build a Digital Model
Design and build (or describe in detail) a simulation of something real. Identify the variables, the conditions, and what you abstracted away. If you can code it, code it (Python, Scratch, JavaScript, even a clear spreadsheet model). If not, sketch it carefully. Reflect on what your model gets right, where it fails, and one ethical question it raises.
TRACK B
pulls from units 2, 4 & 5
💡 Pitch a CS Solution
Identify a real problem in your community — at school, at home, on a team, in your neighborhood. Design (don't necessarily build) a software solution. Use the engineering design process from Week 30: criteria, constraints, prototype sketches. Include an ethical-review pass: who could be harmed, who's left out, and where a human stays in the loop.
TRACK C
pulls from units 4 & 5
🎤 AI Perspectives, Expanded
Build on the Week 33 AI project. Pick a focused question about AI's role in society and take a clear position. Deliver it as a 5-minute presentation (recorded or live) or a 1000-word essay. Defend your position with three pieces of evidence, address one counterargument honestly, and tie your case to one of the four cardinal virtues.
5-Part Structure (every track)

Whichever track you pick, your deliverable should contain these five pieces. They map onto each track a little differently — but they're always there.

1
Your question (or position)
Track A: the question your model answers. Track B: the problem you're solving. Track C: the question you're taking a position on. Keep it focused and specific.
2
Three supports — or three design steps
Track A: three variables and one IF/THEN condition. Track B: three steps of the design process (criteria, constraints, prototype). Track C: three pieces of evidence for your position.
3
A counterargument — or a trade-off
Name what your project gives up or gets wrong. Track A: one detail you abstracted away that could matter. Track B: one trade-off in your design. Track C: one fair counter to your position and your response.
4
An ethical pass
Who could be helped? Who could be harmed? Who's left out? Where should a human stay in the loop? Even a few sentences here separates a strong capstone from a thin one.
5
A virtue connection
Tie your work to one of the four cardinal virtues — prudence, justice, fortitude, or temperance. How does this project make a person more of one of those virtues? One paragraph is enough.
Evaluation Rubric — 25 points total
Criterion Points
Clarity of question / problem / position5
Quality of supports — evidence, design steps, or model details8
Honest counterargument or trade-off acknowledgment4
Ethical pass — who is helped, harmed, or left out4
Polish & virtue connection — readable, on-brand, with a clear classical tie4

An "A" capstone (22+) is one a stranger could grasp in five minutes and would want to talk about afterward. A "B" capstone (17–21) covers all five parts but rushes one or two. Anything below that is missing a required piece — recheck against the structure above before submitting.

Track-specific hints
Track A — Build a model
  • Start with two or three variables. Don't try to model everything.
  • Show one IF/THEN condition explicitly. Make it concrete.
  • Name one detail you deliberately abstracted away — and one detail your model gets wrong.
  • Acceptable tools: Python, Scratch, JavaScript, a Google Sheet, even a careful written description.
Track B — Pitch a CS solution
  • The problem must be real and specific. "Phones are addictive" is too broad. "My grandfather can't read text messages because the font is too small in default settings" is real.
  • List 3 criteria (what the solution must do) and 3 constraints (what limits the design).
  • Sketch a paper prototype. A simple wireframe in any tool is fine — even pen and paper, photographed.
  • You don't have to build it. A clear design, ethically reviewed, is the deliverable.
Track C — AI perspectives, expanded
  • This is more ambitious than the Week 33 project — longer, more researched, with stronger evidence.
  • Ideal length: ~1000 words written OR ~5 minutes spoken.
  • Cite where evidence comes from — even "(Week 32 reading)" is fine for course material.
  • Steelman the counterargument. Make the case against you as strong as you honestly can — then answer it.
Polish Tips
  • Start small and iterate. Going around the cycle twice beats trying to nail it the first time.
  • Show your thinking, not just your output. Reasoning beats decoration.
  • Get one honest piece of feedback before submitting. Watch the listener's face — confusion is information.
  • Read your deliverable out loud. If you stumble, your audience will too.
  • Polish last. Don't pick fonts on day one — get the substance right first.
How to Submit

Upload a single file to your teacher's submission link — PDF, slide deck, document, video, screen recording, or zipped project folder. If your file is huge, share a Google Drive / OneDrive link instead. If you wrote code, include a link to the running version (Repl.it, GitHub Pages, etc.) plus a short write-up of what it does.

This activity is marked complete when you've reviewed the guide and made your plan. Your teacher will assess the capstone deliverable itself separately.

One Last Thing

A capstone, in architecture, is the stone that locks the arch into place. Set yours carefully. The work you do this final week belongs to you for the rest of your life — and the habits you forged building it will outlast the project itself. Go well.

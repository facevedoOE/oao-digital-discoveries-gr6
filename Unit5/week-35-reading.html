UNIT 5 • WEEK 35 • READING

Careers in Computing & Digital Citizenship

Every app you opened today, every traffic light you passed, every game you played — someone built it. Computer science isn't one career. It's a family of careers, each with its own kind of work, its own kind of training, and its own kind of responsibility. This week you'll meet that family — and you'll see why being a good citizen of the digital world is the most important "career skill" of all.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

1
Identify at least six different careers in computing and describe what each one does day to day.
2
Recognize multiple paths into the field — degree, bootcamp, self-teaching, mentorship, apprenticeship — and the common thread that runs through all of them.
3
Name an ethical responsibility unique to each major CS field — not just AI, but accessibility, security, and honest data work.
4
Apply the four cardinal virtues — prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance — to your own life online.
01
Section 1 · The Hidden Engineers Of Your Day

Someone built every machine you touched today

Stop and trace one ordinary morning. The alarm on your phone? A programmer wrote that. The icon you tapped to turn it off? Designed by a person who thought hard about what your tired thumb would look for first. The data that flowed when your weather app showed today's forecast? Routed across the planet by routers programmed by network engineers. The car your family drove past? Its anti-lock brakes ran software written and tested by a team of engineers you'll never meet.

None of these tools made themselves. Behind every one of them is a person — sometimes a single person, often a team — whose vocation is computer science. Vocation is a classical word for a calling: not just a job that pays the bills, but work that suits who you are and serves others. The hidden engineers of your day chose this calling. Many of them are the people who built the world you take for granted.

This week we're going to make that hidden work visible. Some of you reading this will become one of those people. All of you will spend the rest of your lives using what they build. Either way, you should know the family you're meeting.

📘 Vocabulary

Vocation — work you feel called to do, not just paid to do. From the Latin vocare, "to call."

Computer science (CS) — the field of work that designs and builds the software, data, and systems that run modern life.

Career path — the route you take to grow from beginner to skilled practitioner of a vocation.

02
Section 2 · The Map Of CS Careers

Eight callings under one big tent

"Computer science career" isn't one job — it's a family of jobs, each with a different feel. Some are deeply visual. Some are mostly math. Some are about catching bad actors. Some are about helping people who otherwise can't use what the rest of us use easily. Here's the map.

🛠️ Software Engineer
Builds the apps, websites, and tools you use every day. Writes code, fixes bugs, works with teammates to ship features. The "backbone" career — most large companies have many of them.
📊 Data Scientist
Finds patterns in big piles of data. Helps companies, hospitals, and cities answer "what's happening?" and "what might happen next?" Heavy on math, statistics, and curiosity.
🎨 UX Designer
UX = "user experience." Designs how an app or website feels to use. Thinks about every button, every word, every step a user takes. Half psychology, half design, all about empathy.
🔒 Cybersecurity Analyst
Protects systems from being broken into or stolen from. Hunts vulnerabilities, watches for attacks, and patches the holes before bad actors find them. Detective work for the digital age.
🤖 AI / ML Engineer
Builds the AI models that recommend videos, recognize faces, transcribe speech, and drive cars. Knows the math behind machine learning and the ethics behind deploying it.
🦾 Robotics Engineer
Builds machines that move in the physical world — surgical robots, factory arms, warehouse rovers, planetary explorers. Combines code with mechanical and electrical engineering.
🎮 Game Developer
Designs and builds the games you play. A mix of programmer, storyteller, artist, and physics teacher — depending on the role. Teams range from one indie maker to studios of thousands.
🌐 IT / Network Administrator
Keeps an organization's computers, networks, and servers running. Every company has someone on this — when the Wi-Fi works at school, it's because someone like this is doing their job well.

03
Section 3 · How People Actually Get There

More paths than you'd think — and one thing they share

You might picture the road into computer science as one road: high school → college → CS degree → first job. That road exists, and lots of people travel it. But it's not the only road. Real engineers come from real lives, and those lives don't all look the same.

1
The college degree path
Four years of university studying computer science, math, and engineering. Strong for theory and for getting into research or large companies that hire from college campuses. Costs time and money — but for many people, it's the right route.
2
The bootcamp path
An intensive 3–9 month program focused on specific job-ready skills, like building websites or analyzing data. Often used by people switching careers from something else. Less broad than a degree, but faster and far cheaper.
3
The self-taught path
Free online courses, library books, YouTube tutorials, a notebook full of practice problems, and lots of patience. Many great engineers learned this way. The hard part isn't the material — it's the discipline to keep going without a teacher pushing you.
4
The apprenticeship / mentor path
Some companies and military programs hire people without degrees and train them on the job, paired with an experienced engineer. You earn while you learn. Increasingly common — and increasingly respected.
5
The internship pipeline
An internship is a short, paid (or sometimes unpaid) job — usually a summer — where a student does real work for a real company. Most professional engineers had at least one. Internships turn classroom learning into the real thing.

Different roads, same destination. What do they all share? Practice on real problems. Every engineer you've ever met — the famous ones at huge companies and the quiet ones at your local hospital — got there the same way: by solving lots of problems, badly at first, then better, with the courage to keep going when something didn't work.

💡 Did You Know?

Some of the most famous people in computing never finished a CS degree. Margaret Hamilton — the engineer who wrote the software that landed astronauts on the Moon in 1969 — was self-taught after college (her degree was in mathematics, not CS). She invented the term "software engineering" because the field didn't even have a name yet. Sometimes the path doesn't exist until you walk it.

04
Section 4 · Ethical Responsibility — In Every Career

Every CS career has a moral edge — not just AI

You've spent the last few weeks thinking about AI ethics. But ethical responsibility isn't only the AI engineer's problem. Every career in this family has its own moral edge — its own decisions where doing the work well means doing it rightly. Three quick examples.

🎨 UX Designer · Accessibility
A UX designer who makes a button only readable in tiny gray text has excluded people with low vision. A designer who insists on a high-contrast, screen-reader-friendly version has included them. That choice — small, daily, repeated — is what accessibility means in practice.
🔒 Cybersecurity · Honesty
A security engineer who discovers a flaw faces a real choice: report it and help fix it, or sell it to people who want to abuse it. The right answer is obvious — but only if you've made up your mind before the moment arrives.
📊 Data Scientist · Honest Charts
A data scientist can stretch a chart's y-axis to make a tiny change look huge — and tell a true number while telling a false story. The ethics of data work is the ethics of not lying with shapes, colors, and selection.
🛠️ Software Engineer · Quality
An engineer who ships code that "works on my machine" but breaks for thousands of users hasn't really finished the job. Caring about other people using your code — testing it, fixing the edge cases — is the daily ethics of software work.
🌎 Real-World Connection

In 2014, security researcher Heartbleed-discoverer Neel Mehta found a critical flaw in a widely used piece of internet software. He could have kept it quiet, sold it, or used it. Instead he reported it — and within days, engineers across the world patched servers that protected billions of people. One person's ethical choice protected the digital lives of nearly everyone reading these words. Ethical responsibility scales.

05
Section 5 · Digital Citizenship As Virtue

You don't have to be an engineer to live online well

Most of the people reading this will not become full-time computer scientists. But every one of you will spend the rest of your life inside the digital world other people built. How will you live there? The classical tradition has a name for this question — it's the question of virtue. A virtue is a settled habit of acting well. The Greeks and Romans named four cardinal virtues at the center of a good life. Each one has a clear meaning online.

⚖️ Prudence — Wise Foresight
The prudent poster pauses before publishing. Could this hurt someone? Would I be proud of this in five years? Is this true? Prudence is the slow breath before the click. It is the first virtue in classical lists because every other one needs it.
⚖️ Justice — Giving What Is Owed
The just commenter is honest. Credits the source. Doesn't pile onto a stranger's bad day. Defends the absent. Calls out lies without becoming a liar. Justice online is the quiet practice of treating people as people, not pixels.
🛡️ Fortitude — Courage In Hardship
The fortitudinous user keeps learning when it's hard. Faces an inbox of criticism without folding. Tells the truth when a crowd wants something easier. Fortitude online is also the courage to log off when staying on would harm you.
⏳ Temperance — Self-Mastery
The temperate user knows what is enough. Enough scrolling. Enough screen time. Enough opinions shared today. Temperance is not "no fun online" — it is being the one who chooses, not the algorithm.
💭 Think About It — Citizenship As Vocation

For the classical thinkers, being a good citizen was itself a calling — perhaps the most important one. Aristotle wrote that a city's worth was measured by the virtue of its citizens, not the strength of its walls. The digital world is now a kind of city — a vast one, with neighborhoods better and worse than most physical ones. Being a good citizen of that city is everyone's vocation, whether you ever write a line of code or not.

📚 Key Vocabulary — Week 35

Software engineer
A person whose vocation is building apps, websites, and software tools — the most common CS career.
Data scientist
A person who finds patterns and answers in large amounts of data, using math and statistics.
UX designer
A person who designs how a digital product feels and flows for the people using it. UX = user experience.
Cybersecurity
The practice of protecting computers, networks, and data from being broken into, stolen, or misused.
AI / ML engineer
A person who builds and trains artificial-intelligence and machine-learning models for real applications.
Internship
A short, real-work job — usually a summer — that helps a student turn classroom skills into professional ones.
Accessibility
Designing software so people with different abilities — vision, hearing, motor, cognitive — can use it just as well.
Digital citizenship
The habit of acting with virtue — prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance — in your digital life.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q1. Which is the BEST example of ASYNCHRONOUS communication?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) A live phone call
     ❌ Phone calls are synchronous.
  B) A live video chat
     ❌ Video chat is synchronous — happens in real time.
  C) An email you send tonight, friend reads tomorrow            ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Asynchronous — sender and reader don't need to be
        available at the same moment. (Unit 1)
  D) Two people talking face to face
     ❌ Face to face is synchronous.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q2. When you send a video clip across the internet, the data is
    MOST likely:
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Sent as one giant file along a single fixed path
     ❌ The internet doesn't work that way.
  B) Split into packets that travel different paths and are
     reassembled at the destination                              ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Packets hop between routers along different paths and
        reassemble at the destination. (Unit 1)
  C) Hand-carried by a network technician
     ❌ Data travels electronically as packets.
  D) Stored on your phone until friend manually downloads it
     ❌ Sending = real transmission, not just storage.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q3. Friend replies "k." to your long message. Most charitable
    way to handle it?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Assume they're angry and stop talking to them
     ❌ Jumping to "they're angry" is the trap text sets.
  B) Tone is hard to read in short texts — ask before
     assuming, or wait until you can talk in person              ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Short texts strip out tone. Check before assuming the
        worst. (Unit 1)
  C) Send "fine, bye then" and end the friendship
     ❌ Escalation in response to a one-letter reply is the
        spiral to avoid.
  D) Decide every "k." in the world definitely means hatred
     ❌ Texts are short and ambiguous — not a hidden code.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q4. URGENT email with misspelled bank URL. Most likely?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Bank just changed its website name
     ❌ Banks don't change URLs by misspelling.
  B) Phishing attempt to steal your login                        ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Urgency + misspelled URL + "click NOW" is the classic
        phishing recipe. Open the real site directly. (Unit 2)
  C) Normal software update message
     ❌ Software updates don't come from outside emails like
        this.
  D) Friend playing a harmless joke
     ❌ This is a phishing pattern, not a joke.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q5. STRONGEST password choice?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) password123
     ❌ On every cracked-password list. Bot would guess it in
        milliseconds.
  B) Your birthday
     ❌ Birthdays are easy to find or guess.
  C) A long unique phrase, different from every other site      ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Long + unique + not reused is the recipe for a strong
        password. (Unit 2)
  D) Your pet's name
     ❌ Often posted publicly. Easy to find.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q6. Stranger online getting roasted for a small mistake. BEST
    response?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Pile on with a clever joke
     ❌ Piling on harms a real person for small social reward.
  B) Screenshot it and post to your own feed
     ❌ Amplifying someone's bad day is the opposite of being
        a good digital citizen.
  C) Don't add to the harm — defend or message kindly if it
     feels right                                                 ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Don't pile on; if appropriate, defend or message
        kindly. Digital citizenship in action. (Unit 2)
  D) Tag five friends to come laugh too
     ❌ Pulling more people into the pile-on harms further.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q7. score = 0, then score = score + 10. What is `score`?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Function
     ❌ Functions are named reusable code blocks.
  B) Loop
     ❌ Loops repeat code; `score` doesn't repeat.
  C) Variable                                                    ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ A variable is a named box holding a value that can
        change. (Unit 3)
  D) Comment
     ❌ Comments are ignored by the computer; `score` is real
        code.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q8. IF temperature > 32 THEN turn on cooler. temperature = 28.
    What happens?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) The cooler turns on
     ❌ Condition requires > 32. 28 is not > 32.
  B) The cooler does NOT turn on                                 ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ 28 is not > 32, so the IF is false and the THEN block
        is skipped. (Unit 3)
  C) Temperature changes to 32
     ❌ IF only checks the value; it doesn't change it.
  D) The program crashes
     ❌ Nothing to crash on. A clean false IF skips the THEN.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q9. A loop is MOST useful when you want to:
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Save the program for later
     ❌ That's a file-save command, not a loop.
  B) Repeat a block of code a number of times, or for each
     item in a list                                              ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Loops repeat — counted or per-item. (Unit 3)
  C) Send a message to another computer
     ❌ Networking, not looping.
  D) Stop the program forever
     ❌ Loops repeat blocks; they don't stop programs.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q10. Program crashes with an error. MOST productive first move?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Delete and start over
     ❌ You'd throw away every clue. Read first.
  B) Read the error message carefully                            ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ The error message is the program's letter to you —
        most of the answer is in it. (Unit 3)
  C) Re-run the same code unchanged
     ❌ Same code + same data = same crash.
  D) Blame the computer
     ❌ The bug is almost always in the code.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q11. A digital model is BEST described as:
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Exact perfect copy
     ❌ Models are simplified.
  B) Simplified version built from numbers + rules to study
     or predict the real thing                                   ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Digital model = numbers + rules, built for study or
        prediction. (Unit 4)
  C) A photograph
     ❌ Photos are static; models can be run.
  D) Hardware shaped like the thing
     ❌ Digital models are code, not hardware.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q12. Modeling snowman melting. BEST example of a variable?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Freezing point of water (0°C)
     ❌ Constant — doesn't change.
  B) Outdoor temperature (changes hour by hour)                  ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Outdoor temperature changes — that's what a variable
        is. (Unit 4)
  C) The value of pi
     ❌ Constant.
  D) Chemical formula for water (H₂O)
     ❌ A fixed fact, not a changing value.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q13. Which step typically comes FIRST in engineering design?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Build the final product
     ❌ Build is much later.
  B) Identify the problem and its criteria/constraints           ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Define the problem and its constraints first.
        (Unit 4, Week 30)
  C) Run a final user test
     ❌ Testing comes after design/prototype.
  D) Ship the product and write marketing copy
     ❌ Shipping is the last step.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q14. AI flags a patient "high risk." Human-in-the-loop:
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Treatment begins automatically
     ❌ That removes the human — the opposite of the principle.
  B) Doctor reviews the flag, talks to patient, decides          ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ AI assists, doctor decides — the textbook human-in-
        the-loop. (Unit 4)
  C) Patient auto-emailed a diagnosis
     ❌ Skips the human conversation real care requires.
  D) Trust AI confidence with no human review
     ❌ Opposite of human-in-the-loop.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q15. AI hiring tool trained on past 90%-one-demographic hires.
     MOST likely problem?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) AI performs perfectly — past data is always trustworthy
     ❌ Past data carries past biases.
  B) AI may carry forward and amplify the bias, treating new
     applicants unfairly                                         ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Trained on biased history, AI repeats and amplifies
        the bias. Textbook AI-ethics problem. (Unit 4)
  C) AI hires at random
     ❌ A trained AI follows patterns it learned.
  D) AI refuses to hire anyone
     ❌ It would carry past patterns, not refuse outright.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q16. Modeling highway traffic. BEST detail to abstract away?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Cars per minute entering each ramp
     ❌ Critical traffic variable. Keep it.
  B) Number of lanes
     ❌ Directly affects how much traffic the road can carry.
  C) The paint color of each car                                 ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Paint color doesn't affect flow — exactly the kind of
        detail abstraction removes. (Unit 4)
  D) Average speed limit on each road
     ❌ Directly affects traffic speed. Keep it.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q17. Whose job: find patterns in data to answer "what might
     happen next"?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Cybersecurity analyst
     ❌ Cybersecurity is defense, not data analysis.
  B) Data scientist                                              ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Finding patterns in data and predicting what comes
        next is the data scientist's calling. (Unit 5)
  C) UX designer
     ❌ UX shapes how products feel, not data analysis.
  D) Network administrator
     ❌ Admins keep networks running, not analyze data.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q18. Paths into a CS career — which is MOST accurate?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Only a 4-year CS degree
     ❌ Common myth — many paths work.
  B) Multiple paths work; common thread is practice on real
     problems                                                    ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Degree, bootcamp, self-teaching, apprenticeship all
        work; real practice is the through-line. (Unit 5)
  C) Must start before age 10
     ❌ Many engineers start much later.
  D) Internet age made adult learning impossible
     ❌ Internet makes self-teaching easier than ever.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q19. Security engineer finds critical bug. MOST ethical FIRST
     move?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Sell secretly to highest bidder
     ❌ Clear ethical failure — harms users.
  B) Post publicly before company can react
     ❌ Exposes users. Responsible disclosure goes to company
        first.
  C) Report internally and help patch before users harmed       ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Responsible disclosure — report internally first, then
        help fix. (Unit 5)
  D) Use it to peek at colleagues' accounts
     ❌ Abuse of trust. Don't.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q20. You close the app and go finish homework. Which virtue?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Prudence — wise foresight
     ❌ Prudence pauses before deciding. This is mastery in
        the moment.
  B) Justice — what is owed
     ❌ Justice is about others; this is about yourself.
  C) Temperance — knowing what is enough                        ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Temperance is the virtue of knowing your limit and
        choosing it. (Unit 5)
  D) Fortitude — courage in hardship
     ❌ Helps, but the lead virtue is temperance.

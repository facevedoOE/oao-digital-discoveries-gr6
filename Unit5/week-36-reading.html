Section 1 · The Year In One Breath

Five units, one arc

A 36-week year of learning is a lot. But every long thing has a shape, and this course has a shape too. Five units, each building on the last, each adding one new way to think clearly about the digital world.

Unit 1 · Weeks 1–8

How messages move across the internet — packets, routers, addresses; the difference between sync and async; the strange new etiquette of life on screens.
Unit 2 · Weeks 9–18

Who builds the digital world — and how each career carries its own ethics. How the four cardinal virtues turn ordinary online life into a kind of citizenship. And then: your own work, in this capstone.

Section 2 · What This Course Was Really About

A habit, not a vocabulary list

If a stranger asked you what you learned this year, you could list the topics — packets, passwords, Python, machine learning. You'd be right, but you'd also be selling yourself short. Beneath every unit, this course has been teaching a single habit: making thoughtful, careful, virtuous decisions with technology.

Look back at the arc. Unit 1 was about how messages move — and behind that, how to choose the right channel for the right moment. Unit 2 was about how to keep yourself safe — and behind that, how to treat other people online. Unit 3 was about code — and behind that, the patience to read your own mistakes carefully. Unit 4 was about modeling — and behind that, the prudence to abstract well and the wisdom to keep a human in the loop. Unit 5 was about careers and citizenship — and behind that, what kind of person you want to be in this digital city.

Every unit was teaching you to pause and choose well. That's the habit. And it's the one habit you'll carry into every room of your digital life.

💭 Think About It

The classical thinkers had a word for "a habit of choosing well": virtue. A virtue isn't a feeling or a rule — it's a settled habit that shows up under pressure. The student who reflexively pauses before posting has a virtue. The student who has to consult a checklist every time does not yet. Repetition turns choice into habit, and habit into character. This year was a long, slow shaping of that kind of character.

Section 3 · Choosing Your Capstone

Three tracks — pick one that plays to you

Your capstone is the project that crowns the year. It's a chance to show what you've learned — not on a quiz, but in a piece of work you choose, shape, and put your name on. You pick one of three tracks. Each one pulls from different parts of the year. None is harder or better than the others. Choose the one that plays to your strengths and your interests.

Track A · Build A Digital Model
Design and build (or describe in detail) a simulation of something real.
Pulls from Units 3 and 4. Pick something in the world — a snowball fight, a school cafeteria line, the spread of a rumor, a basketball shot. Identify the variables, the conditions, what you abstracted away. If you can code it, code it. If you can't, sketch it clearly. Reflect honestly on what your model gets right and where it fails.
Track B · Pitch A CS Solution
Identify a real problem in your community and design a software solution.
Pulls from Units 2, 4, and 5. Find a real problem someone you know actually has. Use the engineering design process from Week 30 — criteria, constraints, sketches, prototypes. Add an ethical-review pass: who could be harmed? Who's left out? You don't have to build the software — but design it well enough that someone else could.
Track C · AI Perspectives, Expanded
Take a position on AI's role in society and argue it well.
Pulls from Units 4 and 5. Build on the Week 33 project. Pick a focused question about AI and society. Take a clear position. Defend it with three pieces of evidence. Steelman one counterargument. Connect your position to one of the four cardinal virtues. Deliver it as a 5-minute presentation or a 1000-word essay.
🌎 Real-World Connection

"Capstone" comes from architecture: the capstone is the final, top stone placed on an arch or a wall. It locks the rest of the stones together — until the capstone is set, the structure is not yet a structure. Your year of learning is a kind of arch. The next two weeks set the capstone.

Section 4 · Tips From Past Projects

How to make this capstone strong

Every year, students do this kind of capstone. Year after year, the patterns of what makes a strong project — and what sinks a weak one — look the same. Here is the advice past students wish they'd had.

1
Start small. Then iterate.
Don't try to model the whole solar system; model one planet. Don't try to fix every problem in your community; pick the one you understand best. Remember iteration from Week 30 — going around the cycle again and again is the move, not a sign of failure.
2
Show your thinking, not just your output.
A clean output with no reasoning behind it is weaker than a messy output with clear reasoning. Show what you tried, what you ruled out, and why. Your process is half the project.
3
Get one honest piece of feedback before you finish.
Show your draft to a parent, sibling, or friend who isn't in this course. Watch their face. If they're confused, your audience will be. If they ask a real question, write down the answer somewhere in your project.
4
Polish last, not first.
Don't pick fonts on day one. Get the substance right, then make it pretty. Polish on weak content is just decorated thin air.
5
Connect it to a virtue.
The strongest capstones name why the work matters — usually by tying it back to one of the four cardinal virtues. Why is your project a prudent one? A just one? A temperate one? Make that connection explicit somewhere.
💡 Did You Know?

The word capstone shares a root with cap — what goes on top. In medieval Europe, the master mason kept the most beautiful and trickiest stone for last. If the capstone was set well, the arch would stand for a thousand years. If it slipped, the whole thing could come down. A small piece, at the end, holding everything together. That's roughly what this final project is for your year.

Section 5 · What Comes After This Course

Keep building, keep questioning, keep practicing

In a few weeks, this course will be in your past. But the digital world won't be. Whatever you do next — 7th grade computer science, a different elective, a science track, a future career in something completely different — three habits will keep paying off.

Keep building. Pick small projects of your own. A weather widget. A budget tracker. A game. A model of something you care about. The students who keep their hands on the work between courses are the ones who arrive in the next course already ahead.

Keep questioning. When a new app, a new AI tool, a new social platform shows up — and they will keep showing up — bring this year's questions to it. What's it built on? What variables does it track? Who benefits? Who's left out? Where's the human in the loop?

Keep practicing the virtues. Pause before posting. Tell the truth in charts and in comments. Log off when you've had enough. Show up well for the people you share digital space with. These small, repeated choices are how character is made.

💭 A Last Word From The Classical Tradition

Aristotle wrote that excellence is not an act, but a habit — that we are what we repeatedly do. Plato thought the highest aim of education was to teach people to love what is good, so they would freely choose it for the rest of their lives. The Roman thinker Marcus Aurelius reminded himself, every morning, that the day would bring difficult people and difficult choices — and that the right answer was always to act with prudence, justice, fortitude, and temperance.

These ideas are 2,000 years old. They were not written for the internet. They have not been improved upon for the internet. They are the inheritance you take with you. Now, in your own time and your own way, you carry that inheritance forward into a world neither Aristotle nor Marcus Aurelius could have imagined — and you use it well.

📚 Key Vocabulary — Week 36

Capstone
The final, top stone of an arch — and, in school, the culminating project that locks a course of study together.
Synthesis
Pulling together pieces from different places and combining them into a single coherent whole.
Portfolio
A collection of finished work that shows what you can do — kept and added to over time.
Mastery
Deep, settled skill — the kind that shows up easily under pressure, not just on a good day.
Vocation
A calling — work you feel made to do, not just paid to do. From the Latin vocare, "to call."
Citizenship
Belonging to a community — and acting like it. Online, this means treating other users as fellow members of a real place.
Deliverable
The thing you actually hand over at the end — the file, the video, the document. The visible proof of your work.
Reflection
Looking back on what you did and asking what worked, what didn't, and what you'd change. The closing move of every project.

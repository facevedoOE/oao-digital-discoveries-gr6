Digital Communication in Practice

Last week you learned the what of digital communication. This week: the how — writing with clarity, choosing the right tone, and practicing the classical virtues of prudence and temperance every time you hit send.

◆ Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

1
Apply online etiquette rules (the "netiquette" code) to school and personal messages.
2
Write with clarity and concision — and adjust tone to match your audience.
3
Use a reflection rubric to self-assess your own digital messages before sending.
4
Connect digital conduct to the classical virtues of prudence (wise choice) and temperance (self-control).
01
Section 1 · Online Etiquette

Netiquette: the unwritten rules of online conversation

When you walk into a classroom, you already know certain unwritten rules: raise your hand, listen when others speak, don't shout over the teacher. Online spaces have their own unwritten rules — we call them netiquette, a blend of "internet" and "etiquette."

Netiquette exists because online communication is missing something powerful: your face, your voice, your body language. A joke that would get a laugh in person can look mean in a text. A quick comment can feel harsh without a smile attached. Good netiquette fills those gaps on purpose.

01Remember there is a human on the other side.
Before you post or send, picture the reader's face. Would you say it to them in person?
02Don't type in ALL CAPS.
ALL CAPS reads like shouting. Use caps sparingly — and never for a whole message.
03Proofread before you send.
Typos change meaning. A two-second reread can save a big misunderstanding.
04Respect privacy — yours and others'.
Don't share screenshots, passwords, or private info without permission. Ever.
05When you disagree, disagree with ideas — not people.
"I see this differently because…" beats "You're wrong." Kindness is not weakness.
📘 Vocabulary

Netiquette — the unwritten rules of respectful, effective online communication. Short for "network etiquette."

Tone — the attitude or feeling that your words carry; can be formal, casual, friendly, urgent, or cold.

Audience — the person or group receiving your message. Your audience shapes your tone.

Section 2 · The 3 C's of Effective Writing

Clear. Concise. Courteous.

Every strong digital message passes three tests. Writers from ancient Rome to modern newsrooms have relied on versions of these rules. Learn them, and your writing will stand out.

🎯 The 1st C · Clarity
Can the reader understand it on the first read?
Say exactly what you mean. Avoid vague words like "stuff," "thing," or "it" when something specific would be clearer. Good test: if you handed this to a stranger, would they know what you wanted?
✂️ The 2nd C · Concision
Did you respect the reader's time?
Cut every word that doesn't earn its place. Concision isn't rudeness — it's care for the reader. Short messages are usually better than long ones, but never at the cost of clarity.
🤝 The 3rd C · Courtesy
Is the tone kind and appropriate?
"Please" and "thank you" are short words with enormous power. A courteous message opens doors. A rude one closes them — even if every fact inside is correct.
🔧 Before & After · A Teacher Email
❌ Before
"hey so like i dont get the thing we did in class can u just tell me what it is thx"
✅ After
"Hi Mrs. Lee, I'm a bit unclear on the decimals activity from today's lesson. Could you share the example we worked on? Thank you! — Alex"

Same basic request — but clearer (names the specific topic), more concise (one question, not three), and courteous (greeting, "thank you," signature).

✅ Check for Understanding

Which of the 3 C's do you think is the hardest to practice? Why? Pick one and think of a time your message failed that test — then mentally rewrite it.

Section 3 · Matching Tone to Audience

The same message, three different voices

A skilled writer reads the room. You wouldn't use the same tone at a pizza party as at a school awards ceremony. Online, you still need to read the room — the room is just invisible.

Audience
"I can't come to practice today"
👨‍🏫 Coach or teacher
"Hi Coach, I won't be able to attend practice today because of a family commitment. I'll be there Wednesday. Thank you for understanding. — Alex"
👥 Teammate (peer)
"Hey, can't make practice today — family thing. Catch you Wednesday!"
👪 A close family member
"skipping practice today 😅 see u tonight"

Same information — but the tone shifts to match the relationship. With a coach, we're formal and respectful. With a peer, we're friendly and brief. With family, we can be casual and playful.

💭 Think About It

Aristotle said that good speech lives in the mean between extremes — not too stiff, not too loose. Tone is a skill of balance: too formal feels cold, too casual feels disrespectful. Matching tone to audience is how you find the middle.

Section 4 · The "Before You Send" Rubric

A 5-second self-assessment before you hit send

The best digital communicators don't write perfect messages on the first try. They reread. Here's the rubric professionals use — adapted for you:

☑️ Before You Send
1
Clarity check. Could a stranger understand this message without asking a question? If not, rewrite.
2
Concision check. Is there a word I can cut without losing meaning? (There almost always is.)
3
Tone check. Does the tone fit the audience — teacher, peer, family?
4
Courtesy check. Did I include a greeting, "please" or "thank you," and a sign-off when needed?
5
Emotion check. Am I writing this while angry or upset? If yes — wait. Come back in an hour.
💡 Did You Know?

Many professional email apps now offer a "Undo Send" button — usually giving you 5 to 30 seconds to cancel a message. It exists because even the most experienced adults send things they wish they hadn't. The best habit, though, is to write it, wait, reread it, and then send.

Section 5 · Prudence & Temperance Online

Ancient virtues, modern screens

Classical philosophers identified four cardinal virtues — foundational habits of good character: prudence, justice, fortitude, and temperance. Two of them are tested every single day by how you behave online.

⚖️ Prudence
Practical wisdom.
The ability to choose the right action at the right moment. Online, prudence asks: "Is now the right time to send this? Is this the right channel? Is this the right audience?"
🛡️ Temperance
Self-control.
The discipline to pause before reacting. Online, temperance asks: "Should I hit send in this mood? Should I scroll past this post I disagree with? Should I take a break?"
🌎 Real-World Connection

Every single year, CEOs, athletes, and celebrities lose jobs, trophies, and opportunities because of a single message sent without prudence or temperance. A 30-second mistake can follow someone for years.

The habit you build in 6th grade — pausing before you send — is the same habit that protects professionals later in life.

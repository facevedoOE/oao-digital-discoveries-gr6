📖 Core Content
📡 Section 1: What Is Digital Communication?

Digital communication is any exchange of information that takes place using electronic devices and the internet. This includes sending emails, posting on a class discussion board, video calling a relative in another state, chatting in a gaming app, or even just reacting to a post with an emoji. If it travels through a screen or a network, it counts as digital communication.

At its most basic level, every act of digital communication involves three parts:

  • A sender — the person creating and sending the message.
  • A message — the information, idea, or feeling being shared.
  • A receiver — the person (or people) getting the message.

What makes digital communication unique is the channel — the technology that carries the message from one place to another. Choosing the right channel for your message is one of the most important skills a digital communicator can have.

🌐 Section 4: How Messages Travel — Networks Explained

Have you ever wondered how your message goes from your device to your friend's device in less than a second? It all comes down to networks. A network is a system of connected devices that can share information. When you send a digital message, it is broken into small pieces called data packets that travel through routers — devices that act like digital traffic directors — and are reassembled instantly at the other end.

1
Your message is broken into data packets
Instead of sending the whole message at once, your device splits it into small chunks of data called packets.
2
Packets travel through routers
Routers act like digital traffic directors — they read each packet's destination and send it along the fastest available route.
3
Packets are reassembled at the destination
When all packets arrive, they are put back together in order — and your message appears instantly!

TermDefinition

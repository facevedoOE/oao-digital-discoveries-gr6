◆ The Big Ideas of Unit 1

If you remember just four things from Unit 1, make it these:

1
Match the channel to the message. Synchronous vs asynchronous. Formal vs casual. The right tool for the right audience and moment.
2
Multimedia design starts with purpose and audience. Inform, persuade, or entertain — and design every choice for a real, specific viewer.

L1
Lesson 1 Recap · Digital Communication (Weeks 1–2)

Channels, tone, and the wisdom to pause

📡 Communication Channels
  • Synchronous = real time (video calls, live chats). Asynchronous = on your own time (email, discussion board). The Greek a- means "not."
  • Five tool families: email, messaging, video calls, discussion boards, collaborative documents. Each has a "best fit."
  • Messages travel as data packets through routers, getting reassembled at the destination — sometimes from different paths.
✏️ Writing Well Online
  • The 3 C's: Clarity. Concision. Courtesy.
  • Tone matches audience — formal for teachers, casual with peers.
  • The Before You Send rubric: clarity check, concision check, tone check, courtesy check, emotion check.
  • Classical virtues: prudence (right action, right time) and temperance (self-control, especially when feelings run hot).
L2
Lesson 2 Recap · Multimedia Design (Weeks 3–4)

Plan, build, integrate, share

🎯 Purpose, Audience, Plan
  • Multimedia = two or more media types (text, image, audio, video) on purpose.
  • Aristotle's three rhetorical purposes: inform, persuade, entertain.
  • 5 audience questions: age, prior knowledge, what they care about, where they'll see it, how much time they'll give it.
  • Storyboard first. Sketches are cheap to change; finished work is expensive to redo.
🎨 Build & Critique
  • Aesthetic unity: a 3–5 color palette + a 2-font pair + consistent layout, used across every page.
  • Integration: text, visuals, and audio all support the SAME idea (not fighting each other).
  • Collaboration protocols: divide pages by name, lock in design system first, comment instead of silently overwriting.
  • Glow & Grow peer feedback: name one specific strength, name one specific improvement.
  • The 6-point quality checklist before "shipping": purpose, audience, aesthetic unity, integration, proofread, credit.
L3
Lesson 3 Recap · Files & Software (Weeks 5–6)

The bones of digital work

📁 Files
  • 5 file families: Documents (.docx, .pdf, .txt) · Images (.jpg, .png, .svg) · Audio (.mp3, .wav) · Video (.mp4, .mov) · 3D (.stl, .obj).
  • The file extension tells the computer what kind of file it is. Don't strip it.
  • 4 naming rules: be specific, no spaces (use hyphens), date/version it, keep the extension.
  • A folder hierarchy groups related files; saves future-you hours of searching.
⚙️ Software
  • Word = strong layout + offline. Google Docs = built-in real-time co-editing + cloud sync.
  • Cloud storage lives in real data centers — and lets one file follow you across every device.
  • 4 collaboration features: real-time co-editing · comments · suggesting mode · version history (lifesaver after accidents).
  • Every tool has tradeoffs. The smart move is matching the tool to the job.
🏆 Unit 1 Mini-Project

The "Digital Foundations" Mini-Multimedia Project

Build a short multimedia piece — poster, slide deck, or infographic — that teaches a younger student (a 3rd or 4th grader) one thing you learned in Unit 1. You'll use every skill you've practiced.

📋 Project Requirements
  • Pick one Unit 1 topic. Examples: "What is a phishing email?" "How do messages travel?" "What is netiquette?" "What's the difference between a JPG and a PDF?"
  • Identify your audience. A 3rd or 4th grader who has never heard of this topic.
  • Name your purpose. Mainly to inform, with a touch of entertain if you want.
  • Storyboard first. Sketch 3–5 panels on paper or in a doc before you build.
  • Build in Canva, Google Slides, or Word. Use a 3–5 color palette and 2-font pair throughout.
  • Integrate at least 2 media types — text + visual at minimum.
  • Name your file using all 4 naming rules. Export as a PDF for submission.
  • Get peer feedback. Trade with a classmate or family member: one Glow, one Grow.
  • Run the 6-point quality checklist before submitting.

Section 1 · From Storyboard to Build

Meet Canva — your design workshop

Canva is a free, browser-based design tool used by students, teachers, and professionals to create posters, slide decks, infographics, social posts, short videos, and more. Think of it as a digital workshop with thousands of templates and ready-made shapes, fonts, and photos. Your storyboard is the blueprint; Canva is where you build.

🔧 The 4-Step Build Workflow
1
Choose your project type
Poster? Slides? Infographic? Pick the format that matches your purpose and audience. Canva offers templates for each.
2
Set up your design system
Pick 2–3 fonts and 3–5 colors at the start. This is the secret to aesthetic unity.
3
Build page-by-page from your storyboard
Open your storyboard. Build one page at a time. Don't skip ahead — finishing pages in order keeps you organized.
4
Review, revise, export
Step away. Come back. Read it like an outsider. Fix what needs fixing. Then export as PDF or video.
📘 Vocabulary

Template — a pre-made starting design you can customize with your own content.

Design system — the small set of colors, fonts, and styles you commit to using across a project.

Export — turning your finished design into a sharable file (PDF, image, video).

Section 2 · Aesthetic Unity

Why does it look like one thing?

Aesthetic unity is the feeling that every page of a design belongs together — that it was made by one designer, with one taste, for one project. Without unity, even good content looks unprofessional. With it, even simple content looks polished.

🎨 The 3 Tools of Aesthetic Unity
1. A Color Palette (3–5 colors max)
Pick a small set of colors and use them everywhere. The OAO brand palette uses these:

#C7922C
Gamer Green

2. A Font Pairing (2 fonts max)
Pick one font for headings (bold, attention-grabbing) and one for body text (clean, easy to read). Don't add a third — three fonts almost always feels chaotic.
Headings: Wix Madefor Display
Body: Wix Madefor Text — easy to read in long passages, with a friendly modern feel that pairs nicely with the bolder display heading above.
3. Consistent Layout
Use the same margins, the same heading position, and the same way of placing images on every page. The viewer should never feel "lost" between pages.
💡 Did You Know?

Big companies like Nike, Disney, and Apple all keep brand guidelines — long documents that lock down their colors, fonts, and styles. Optima Academy has one too! When you saw Binary Blue and Gateway Gold above, you saw real OAO brand colors. Designers at every major studio use the same trick: commit to a small system, and everything looks unified.

03
Section 3 · Collaboration Protocols

Two designers, one design — without chaos

Canva, Google Slides, and Google Docs all let multiple people edit the same file at the same time. Powerful — and a recipe for confusion if you don't agree on rules first. Smart teams set collaboration protocols at the start.

📋1. Divide pages by name.
Decide upfront: "Maya owns pages 1–2, Diego owns pages 3–4." No two people editing the same page at once.
🎨2. Agree on the design system FIRST.
Lock in colors, fonts, and layout before anyone builds. Otherwise you'll get four different-looking pages.
💬3. Use comments — don't delete.
If you disagree with a teammate's choice, leave a comment. Don't silently delete or rewrite their work.
⏰4. Set check-in points.
"By Friday, all first drafts done. Saturday: review together." A schedule keeps everyone honest.
🤝5. Credit the team.
List every contributor on the final piece. Even a small "Designed by: Maya, Diego, and Sofia" matters.
💭 Think About It

The classical virtue of justice means giving each person their due. In group work, that means honest credit, fair workload, and respect for everyone's contributions — even the small ones. Justice isn't a courtroom thing; it's a daily collaboration thing.

Section 4 · Peer Feedback

Trade feedback that actually helps

"This is so good!" feels nice — but it doesn't help your classmate make their project stronger. Real peer feedback is specific, kind, and actionable.

✨ Glow
One thing that's working.
"The way you used Gateway Gold for every heading really pulls the whole project together."
🌱 Grow
One thing to improve.
"On page 3, the text is hard to read against the photo — could you add a darker background behind the words?"

The Glow & Grow framework forces specifics. You can't say "I liked it" — you have to name what you liked. You can't say "Make it better" — you have to suggest how.

🔍 Feedback Comparison
❌ Weak
  • "Good job!"
  • "It's fine."
  • "This is bad."
  • "Change everything."
✅ Strong
  • "The bold heading on page 1 grabs attention."
  • "Page 2's photo doesn't match the topic — try a forest image instead."
  • "Three different fonts feel chaotic — pick two."
  • "Add page numbers so the reader can track progress."
✅ Check for Understanding

When a classmate gives YOU feedback, what's the wisest first response — even if you don't agree? Hint: it's not "but actually..."

Section 5 · Final Quality Checklist

Before you call it done

☑️ The 6-Point Quality Checklist
1
Purpose check. Can a stranger tell whether the project is meant to inform, persuade, or entertain?
2
Audience check. Does the project match the people who will see it?
3
Aesthetic unity check. Same colors, same fonts, same layout style across every page?
4
Integration check. Do text, visuals, and audio (if any) all support the same idea?
5
Proofread check. No typos, broken links, or pixelated images?
6
Credit check. Every collaborator listed; any borrowed images/sounds attributed?
🌎 Real-World Connection

Marketing teams, news graphics teams, and game studios all use checklists exactly like this one before publishing. A single typo on a billboard or a single broken image on a website can cost a company thousands of dollars and a lot of trust.

The discipline of running a checklist before "shipping" is what separates a polished pro from a rushed amateur — at any age.

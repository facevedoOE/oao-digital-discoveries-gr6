Data Access & Permissions

Last week you traced where data goes. This week you'll learn who is allowed to see it, edit it, or delete it — and why a bad permission setting can ruin a group project, leak a private photo, or wipe out hours of work in one click.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

1
Define permissions and explain why digital systems use them.
2
Compare common roles — Owner/Admin, Editor, Commenter, Viewer — and choose the right one for a job.
3
Configure sharing settings safely, including who can find a link and what they can do with it.
4
Apply ethical thinking to access — recognizing the difference between what you can see and what you should see.
01
Section 1 · What Are Permissions?

Digital keys to digital rooms

Imagine a school building. The front office has one set of keys. The science lab has a different set. The principal can open every door, but a 6th-grade student can only open their classroom and the cafeteria. Nobody thinks that's strange — different people need different access.

Digital files work exactly the same way. Permissions are the rules that decide who can open, change, share, or delete a piece of data. Without permissions, every file would be like an unlocked classroom — anyone could walk in and rearrange your desk.

📘 Vocabulary

Permission — a rule that decides what someone is allowed to do with a piece of data.

Access control — the system that enforces permissions inside an app or network.

Role — a named bundle of permissions (like "Editor" or "Viewer") that you give to a user all at once.

02
Section 2 · The Big Four Roles

Owner, Editor, Commenter, Viewer

Almost every cloud app — Google Drive, Microsoft 365, Canvas, Dropbox — uses the same four roles, just with slightly different names. Once you know these four, you can use any platform.

👑Owner / Admin
Has every permission. Can view, edit, share, change other people's roles, and delete the file. Real-life: the person who created the doc; the school IT admin who manages everyone's accounts.
✏️Editor
Can view and change the file — type, delete words, add pages. Usually CAN'T change who else has access. Real-life: a teammate working on a shared lab report.
💬Commenter
Can view and add comments or suggestions, but can't change the actual content. Real-life: a teacher giving feedback on your draft without changing your words.
👁️Viewer
Read-only. Can open and read the file but can't change anything or leave a comment. Real-life: a parent who you want to see your finished essay but not edit it.

✅ Check for Understanding

Pause and think: You finish a personal essay and you want your dad to read it and tell you what he thinks — but you don't want him changing your sentences. Which role should you give him: Editor, Commenter, or Viewer? Why?

(Hint: which role can leave feedback but can't change words?)

03
Section 3 · Sharing Settings

"Anyone with the link" is louder than you think

When you click "Share" on a file, you usually get two big choices: who can find this file, and what they can do with it. The first choice is called the share scope, and it usually has three levels:

🔒Specific People
Only the email addresses you list can open it. The safest setting. Use for: personal essays, sensitive group projects, things only certain people should see.
🏫Anyone in Your School (or Org)
Anyone with a school email can find and open it — but the public can't. Medium openness. Use for: a study guide you want to share with your whole class.
🌐Anyone with the Link
Warning — open to the entire internet. If anyone shares the link, anyone in the world can open it. Use only for: truly public information you'd be okay with strangers seeing.
💭 Think About It

There's a simple security idea called the principle of least privilege: give every person exactly as much access as they need to do their job — and not one bit more. The fewer keys floating around, the safer your data. A janitor doesn't need the principal's master key, and a Viewer doesn't need to be an Editor.

04
Section 4 · Managing Permissions Over Time

Permissions aren't "set and forget"

A common mistake: students share a file with a group at the start of a project, then never touch the settings again. Six months later, three people who left the group still have full Editor access. That's a leak waiting to happen.

Smart permission management means doing three things, on a regular schedule:

ADD
Add the right people, with the right role
Before you click "Share," ask: who needs this, and what's the lowest role that lets them do their job?
DROP
Remove people who no longer need access
When the project ends, when a teammate leaves the group, or when a parent stops needing to see your work — take their key back.
CHECK
Audit the access list
An audit is just a check — open the share panel and read the list. You'll be surprised how often someone shouldn't be there anymore.
💡 Did You Know?

Many of the biggest data leaks in real companies have started with one tiny mistake: a folder accidentally set to "Anyone with the link." Banks, hospitals, and even government agencies have leaked thousands of files this way. The fix isn't fancy software — it's people who understand permissions.

05
Section 5 · The Ethics of Access

Just because you CAN see it doesn't mean you SHOULD

Sometimes a friend hands you their phone to show you a video — and their text messages are right there. Sometimes a teammate forgets to close their tab. Sometimes a teacher leaves a folder set to "Anyone with the link" by mistake. Permissions tell you what's technically possible. Ethics tell you what's right.

✅ Wise Choices
  • Ask before you open someone else's file
  • If you stumble onto private data by accident, close it and tell the owner
  • Never share a link that wasn't shared with you to share
  • Treat other people's data like a borrowed book — give it back, don't read the bookmarks
⚠️ Watch Out
  • Snooping in a sibling's open Google Drive is still snooping
  • Forwarding a "Viewer-only" link to people the owner didn't approve breaks trust
  • Editing someone's doc when you only have view-by-mistake access is sneaky
  • "They left it open" isn't permission — it's a mistake
🌎 Real-World Connection

Classical thinkers called the virtue of giving each person their proper due — no more, no less — justice. Permission settings are a small daily practice in justice: the right access for the right role. The same habit that makes you a good lab partner makes a doctor, banker, or government worker trustworthy with millions of records.

Hospitals, banks, and the U.S. military all use the exact same role-based permission systems you're learning to manage in Google Drive — just at a much bigger scale.

🪞 Weekly Reflection
Audit one of your shared files.

Pick one shared file or folder you've created — a Google Doc, a Canvas submission, a shared playlist. Open the share panel and look at the access list. Then answer in 4–6 sentences:

  • Who has access right now? What role does each person have?
  • Is anyone listed who shouldn't be? Anyone who has more access than they need?
  • What's one change you'd make based on the principle of least privilege?

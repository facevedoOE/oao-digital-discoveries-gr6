Evaluating Online Sources

Anyone can publish anything online. A 6th-grade homework helper. A textbook publisher. A scammer in a basement. A bot pretending to be a doctor. This week you'll learn to tell them apart — using a tool real journalists, scientists, and lawyers rely on every day.

◆ Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

1
Apply the CRAAP test — Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose — to any online source.
2
Read a URL — what .gov, .edu, .org, and .com tell you (and don't).
3
Spot fake or AI-generated content using cross-checks and lateral reading.
4
Recognize online scams — clickbait, fake giveaways, fake tech support.
01
Section 1 · The Firehose Problem

More info than ever — and not all of it is true

Every minute, the internet adds enough new content to fill 200,000 books. Some of it is brilliantly accurate. Some is sloppy. Some is wrong on purpose. And now, AI can generate convincing-sounding articles and images in seconds.

A skilled digital citizen doesn't believe everything — and doesn't disbelieve everything either. They evaluate. The good news: the same five questions that journalists, doctors, and judges use to size up a source work just as well for a 6th-grade research project.

📘 Vocabulary

Source — where information comes from (a website, a book, a person, a video).

Author — the specific person (or group) who created the source.

Credentials — the proof that an author actually knows what they're talking about (degrees, jobs, experience).

Bias — a leaning toward one side of an issue. Some bias is honest; some is hidden.

02
Section 2 · The CRAAP Test

Five questions that crack any source open

Librarians invented this checklist for college students, but it works for sixth-graders just as well. The funny name (CRAAP) makes it easy to remember — and the test is genuinely useful. Walk through each letter for any source you're thinking of trusting:

C
Currency — When was it written?
Look for a publication or "last updated" date. Old info isn't always wrong, but for tech, science, or current events, fresh sources usually win.
R
Relevance — Does it actually answer your question?
A page can be high-quality and still be the wrong tool for the job. Make sure the source covers the topic at the right level of depth.
A
Authority — Who wrote it, and what makes them an expert?
Real authors put their name and credentials on their work. "Anonymous" or "Admin" on a topic that needs expertise = caution.
A
Accuracy — Are the facts right? Can you double-check?
Good sources cite where their facts come from. If the article makes big claims with zero sources, be suspicious — and check the same fact on a different trusted site.
P
Purpose — Why does this site exist?
To inform? To sell something? To persuade you politically? To get clicks? Every site has a purpose. Knowing it helps you read between the lines.
✅ Check for Understanding

Pause and think: A blog post about ancient Rome was last updated in 1998. The author lists no name and no credentials. There are zero sources cited. Which TWO letters of CRAAP are flashing red here?

(Hint: read the description twice — there are at least two problems.)

03
Section 3 · Reading the URL

What the address bar is telling you

Before you even read a single word on a page, you can learn a lot from the URL. The last few letters of the domain — called the top-level domain (TLD) — tell you who's allowed to register that kind of address.

🔗 Anatomy of a URL
https://www.smithsonian.edu/exhibits/rome
Domain
The unique name (smithsonian).
Top-Level Domain
The category (.edu = education).
.gov · government
Only U.S. government agencies. Usually high-trust for official rules and statistics. Example: nasa.gov
.edu · education
Only U.S. accredited schools and universities. Often good for academic content. Example: harvard.edu
.org · organization
Originally non-profits — but now anyone can register. Check authority case-by-case. Example: redcross.org
.com · commercial
Anyone can register. Quality is all over the map — could be the New York Times, could be a scam. Use CRAAP.
💭 Think About It

A .gov isn't automatically right, and a .com isn't automatically wrong. The TLD is a clue, not a verdict. Classical reasoning teaches us to weigh evidence carefully — never let a single signal decide the case.

04
Section 4 · Spotting Fakes

Lateral reading and the AI-content problem

Here's a trick real fact-checkers use: instead of staying on a website to judge it, they leave. They open a new tab and search for the website's name plus the word "review" or "owner." This is called lateral reading, and it's one of the most powerful skills you can build this year.

↔️ Lateral Reading in 3 Steps
1
Open a new tab instead of trusting the page in front of you.
2
Search for the source — "is example.com trustworthy" or "[claim] fact check."
3
Read what other sources say about it. If three independent trusted sites agree, the claim is probably solid.
🤖 The AI-Generated Content Challenge

AI tools can now write articles, generate fake "photos," and even fabricate quotes from real people. None of this is automatically bad — but you have to know it's possible.

  • Be extra careful with claims you can't verify on a second site.
  • Watch for AI-image tells: weird hands, melted text, mismatched earrings or shadows.
  • If a quote sounds shocking, search for it — real quotes have a paper trail.
💡 Did You Know?

Stanford researchers studied how professional fact-checkers and college students evaluate websites. The students mostly stayed on the suspicious page, looking for clues. The pros left the page and read about it elsewhere — and they spotted fakes far faster. Lateral reading is now taught in journalism schools across the world.

05
Section 5 · Online Scams

Clickbait, fake giveaways, and the "tech support" trap

Some websites aren't just unreliable — they're designed to trick you. Here are three of the most common scams you'll see online, and the tells that give them away:

🎣Clickbait
Tells: "You won't BELIEVE…", "Doctors HATE this trick…", clickable thumbnails of shocking faces. Move: close the tab. The actual content rarely matches the headline.
🎁Fake Giveaways
Tells: "Free Robux! Free V-Bucks! Just enter your username and password!" Real game companies never give away currency for free, and never ask for your password to do it. Move: close, report.
🛠️Fake Tech Support
Tells: A pop-up screams "YOUR COMPUTER IS INFECTED — call 1-800-FAKE-NOW immediately!" Microsoft and Apple never show pop-ups asking you to call a phone number. Move: close the tab; if it won't close, force-quit the browser.
🌎 Real-World Connection

Doctors do source evaluation when they read medical journals. Lawyers do it when they argue cases. Scientists do it when they publish papers. Even Supreme Court justices apply tests very similar to CRAAP when they decide whether to trust a piece of evidence.

The 6th-grade habit of pausing to ask "who wrote this, why, and how do I know?" is the same habit that keeps democracies, hospitals, and science labs honest.

The person who created the source — and the proof they're qualified to write it.
Credentials
The degrees, jobs, or experience that show an author knows what they're talking about.

Leaving a page to fact-check it on other trusted sources.
Fact-check
Verifying a claim against multiple independent trusted sources.
Clickbait
A shocking or exaggerated headline designed to bait clicks, not to inform.
Citation
A reference to where a fact came from — proof an author isn't just making things up.
🪞 Weekly Reflection
Run the CRAAP test on a real website.

Pick a website you'd use for a school project — anything from Wikipedia to a fan blog to a .gov site. Then answer in 4–6 sentences:

  • Which CRAAP letter does this site pass clearly? Which letter raises a question?
  • What does the URL (especially the TLD) tell you?
  • Would you cite this in an essay? Why or why not?

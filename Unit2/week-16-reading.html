Unit 2 Review & Study Guide

Seven weeks. Three lessons. Six standards. One PSA project. This week you'll bring it all together — the big ideas, the key vocabulary, the recurring patterns — and get ready for the Unit 2 Assessment.

The Big Ideas of Unit 2

If you remember just four things from Unit 2, make it these:

1
Data lives in three states. At rest (stored). In motion (traveling). In use (open in an app). Smart citizens know which state their data is in — and how to protect each one.
2
Right access for the right role. Permissions, share scopes, and the principle of least privilege. The fewer keys floating around, the safer everyone is.
3
Trust, but verify. Phishing tells, CRAAP, lateral reading. Real companies never ask for your password — and a polished site can still be wrong.
4
Tech serves YOU, not the other way around. Strong passwords + MFA + healthy habits + temperance. The best digital citizens choose how technology shapes their lives.

Lesson 4 Recap · Data & Permissions (Weeks 9–10)

Where data sleeps, how it travels, and who's allowed to touch it

📡Week 9 — The Journey of Data (SC.6.CS.1.1)
  • Three states of data: at rest, in motion, in use. Each state matters for protection and cost.
  • Local vs cloud storage: local works without internet; cloud lets you reach files anywhere.
  • Networks scale up: LAN (one place) → WAN (cities/states) → Internet (the whole planet).
  • Servers + databases + queries = how data is stored and retrieved at scale.
🔑Week 10 — Permissions & Access (SC.6.CS.1.2)
  • The four roles: Owner, Editor, Commenter, Viewer — each a different bundle of permissions.
  • Share scopes: Specific people / your school / anyone with the link. Match scope to need.
  • Principle of least privilege: give exactly the access someone needs — no more.
  • Audit access regularly. Old keys are leaks waiting to happen.
  • Ethics of access: just because you CAN see it doesn't mean you SHOULD.

Lesson 5 Recap · Cyber Safety & Identity (Weeks 11–12)

Spotting attackers, protecting passwords, and managing your digital self

🎣Week 11 — Phishing, Social Engineering & Privacy (SC.6.CS.1.3, 1.4, 2.1, HS.1.1)
  • Phishing tells: urgency, sneaky links, bad spelling, generic greetings. Smishing = SMS, vishing = voice calls.
  • Social-engineering levers: urgency, authority, reward/scarcity, friendliness.
  • Three info classes: PUBLIC (safe), PRIVATE (trusted people only), CONFIDENTIAL (never share — like passwords and MFA codes).
  • Strong passwords: long passphrase > clever password. 16+ characters. Unique per account. Use a password manager.
  • MFA / 2FA: a second proof beyond your password. Stops most account takeovers cold.
  • Five safe habits: lock screen, install updates, don't click strange links, careful on public Wi-Fi, tell a trusted adult.
🔐Week 12 — Encryption & Digital Identity (SC.6.CS.1.5, 1.6)
  • Encryption: scrambles a message so only the right person can read it. Plaintext → ciphertext → plaintext.
  • Caesar / shift cipher: shift each letter by a fixed number. Old, simple, breakable — but the seed of all modern crypto.
  • HTTPS: the "s" means secure. Login pages MUST use HTTPS. Never type passwords on plain HTTP.
  • Digital identity: username, avatar, bio, privacy settings. Choose carefully — these last for years.
  • Active vs passive footprint: posts you write (active) vs data collected automatically (passive). The Permanence Rule: assume online = forever.
L6
Lesson 6 Recap · Source Evaluation & Wellness (Weeks 13–14)

Evaluating what you read, and choosing how technology shapes you

🔍Week 13 — Evaluating Online Sources (SC.6.HS.1.2, 1.4)
  • The CRAAP test: Currency. Relevance. Authority. Accuracy. Purpose. Five questions, any source.
  • TLDs: .gov (U.S. government — restricted), .edu (U.S. schools — restricted), .org (anyone), .com (anyone).
  • Lateral reading: leave the page; check what other trusted sources say.
  • Watch for AI content and clickbait: shocking headlines, weird AI image tells, emotionally extreme claims with no source.
  • Common scams: clickbait, fake giveaways, fake tech-support pop-ups. Real companies don't show pop-ups demanding phone calls.
🌱Week 14 — Digital Wellness & Screen Time (SC.6.HS.2.2)
  • Active vs passive screen time: creating/learning/connecting (active) vs scrolling/watching/absorbing (passive).
  • Body effects: sleep (blue light suppresses melatonin), eyes (use the 20-20-20 rule), posture, sedentary behavior.
  • Mind effects: attention erosion, social comparison, doomscrolling.
  • U-shaped curve: a little screen time helps; heavy use hurts. Aim for moderate, intentional, mostly active.
  • Concrete habits beat vague resolutions: phone across the room at 9 p.m. > "use my phone less."

💭 The Big Picture

Three classical virtues run through this whole unit:

  • Prudence (right action, right time): every "should I share this?" or "is this site trustworthy?"
  • Justice (giving each their due): permission settings — the right access for the right role.
  • Temperance (self-control): healthy habits with screens, sleep, and your own attention.

Cyber safety isn't just a tech skill. It's a daily practice in classical character — at digital scale.

Phishing, Social Engineering & Privacy

Last week you learned about giving the right people the right keys. This week meets the people on the other side — the scammers, tricksters, and impersonators trying to steal those keys. Spot the bait, lock your accounts, and keep your private life private.

◆ Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

1
Identify phishing messages and the social-engineering tricks behind them.
2
Classify information as public, private, or confidential — and explain why each kind needs different protection.
3
Build strong passwords (and explain why passphrases and multi-factor authentication matter).
4
Apply safe device habits — locking, updates, and smart public-Wi-Fi behavior.
01
Section 1 · Phishing 101

The bait, the hook, the catch

A scammer sends out thousands of fake messages pretending to be a real company — your bank, your school, a popular game. Most people delete them. But if even one or two people click the link and type in a password, the scammer wins. That's phishing: fishing for victims with fake bait.

The word is spelled with a "ph" because early hackers borrowed it from "phone phreaking" — and now phishing happens by email, text, DM, even fake websites.

🎣 Anatomy of a Phishing Email
From: support@gooogle-secure.com ← misspelled domain
Subject: ⚠️ URGENT: Your account will be locked in 1 hour!!! ← false urgency
Dear Costumer, ← bad spelling, generic greeting
We notice a problem with your account. Click the button below to verify password and avoid suspention.
VERIFY NOW
↑ link goes to googel-account-secure.fakedomain.ru — not Google
Thanks,
Google Team
⏰ Urgency
"Act NOW or lose your account!" — scammers rush you so you don't think.
🔗 Sneaky Links
A link that LOOKS right but actually goes to a fake site. Hover before clicking.
✏️ Bad Spelling
Real companies have editors. Phishers often have typos and weird grammar.
👤 Generic Hi
"Dear customer." Real services usually use your name. "Dear customer" = mass blast.
📘 Vocabulary

Phishing — a scam that uses a fake message (email, text, DM) to trick you into giving up a password or personal info.

Smishing — phishing that comes by SMS text message.

Vishing — phishing that comes by phone call (voice).

02
Section 2 · Social Engineering

Hacking the human, not the computer

Phishing is one type of a bigger family of tricks called social engineering — manipulating people instead of computers. The strongest password in the world doesn't help if a scammer convinces YOU to hand it over. Social engineers usually pull on one of four emotional levers:

⏰ Urgency
"Act in the next 5 minutes!"
Pushes you to skip the careful thinking step. Real institutions almost never demand instant action.
👮 Authority
"This is the IRS / your principal."
Pretends to be someone in charge. Authority makes us feel like we owe a quick answer.
🎁 Reward / Scarcity
"You've won! Limited spots!"
Free gift cards, "exclusive" Roblox skins. The reward is bait — grabbing it is the trap.
😊 Friendliness
"Hey, can you help me real quick?"
A friendly tone — sometimes pretending to be a friend or a classmate — to lower your guard.
✅ Check for Understanding

Pause and think: You get a Discord DM that says "Hey, I'm a Roblox employee — your account is about to be banned. Click here to verify in the next 5 minutes!" Which TWO social-engineering levers is that message pulling on?

(Hint: read it again and look at the words "Roblox employee" and "5 minutes.")

03
Section 3 · Public, Private, Confidential

Three buckets of information — know which is which

Not all data needs the same protection. The first step in keeping yourself safe is being able to classify the information you handle. Most schools, hospitals, and businesses sort information into three buckets:

🌎Public Information
Examples: your school's name, your favorite sports team, your username on a public game leaderboard. Safe to share with anyone — already known or meant to be known.
🔒Private Information
Examples: your home address, your phone number, your birthdate, your school schedule, photos of yourself. Share only with people you actually know and trust.
🛡️Confidential Information
Examples: passwords, Social Security numbers, credit card numbers, medical records, two-factor codes. Never share these — not in chat, not by email, not even with people who claim to be from a company. Real companies will never ask.
💭 Think About It

Information that identifies a real human being — your name combined with your address, your photo, your birthday — is called PII (Personally Identifiable Information). The classical virtue of temperance — self-control — applies here. Every time someone online asks for PII, your job is to pause and think: Do they actually need this?

04
Section 4 · Strong Passwords & MFA

The lock on your digital front door

A password is the lock on the door to every account you own. Cracking a weak password takes a computer about a second. A strong password takes thousands of years. The difference is mostly about length — longer beats clever.

❌ Weak
  • password123
  • yourname2014
  • qwerty
  • Anything with your birthday, pet's name, or "1234"
  • The same password reused across 10 sites
✅ Strong
  • A passphrase: orange-lamp-tiger-cloud-9
  • 16+ characters, mix of letters, numbers, symbols
  • Unique for each account
  • Stored in a password manager — not on a sticky note
  • Backed up by multi-factor authentication
🔐 What is MFA / 2FA?

Multi-factor authentication (MFA), sometimes called two-factor authentication (2FA), is when an account asks for two proofs that you're really you:

  • Something you know — your password.
  • Something you have — a 6-digit code texted to your phone, or generated by an app like Google Authenticator.

Even if a phisher steals your password, they can't get in without that second code. MFA stops the vast majority of account takeovers.

💡 Did You Know?

A modern attacker's computer can guess tens of billions of passwords per second. A 6-character password? Gone in under a second. A 16-character passphrase using random words? It would take longer than the age of the universe.

05
Section 5 · Safe Device Habits

Five small habits that beat most attacks

Most digital safety isn't about being a hacker — it's about a few small habits done every day. These five protect you against the majority of real attacks:

1
Lock your screen
Set your phone and laptop to lock automatically after 1–5 minutes. A lock screen is the cheapest security upgrade in the world.
2
Install software updates
Updates aren't just new features — they patch security holes. Skipping them is like leaving a window open after the locksmith just told you it's broken.
3
Don't click strange links
When in doubt, type the website address yourself in a new tab — don't trust the link in the email.
4
Be careful on public Wi-Fi
Free coffee-shop Wi-Fi can be watched by anyone nearby. Avoid logging into bank or school accounts on it — wait until you're home.
5
Tell a trusted adult when something feels wrong
Got a creepy DM? An email demanding money? An account locked you out? You don't have to figure it out alone — that's what parents, teachers, and IT staff are for.
🌎 Real-World Connection

Most successful attacks on big companies — banks, hospitals, even schools — start with one phishing email opened by one person. Cybersecurity professionals call this "the human layer." It's the layer YOU just learned to defend.

Companies pay six-figure salaries to people whose job is to teach others what you read this week. The 6th-grade habits you build now are the same instincts adults rely on every day.

📚 Key Vocabulary — Week 11
Phishing
A scam that uses fake messages to trick people into giving up passwords or personal info.
Social engineering
Tricks that manipulate people (not computers) into giving up information or access.
Smishing / Vishing
Phishing by SMS text (smishing) or by phone call (vishing).
Public information
Data already known or meant to be known — safe to share.
Private information
Personal details (address, phone, schedule) shared only with people you know and trust.
Confidential information
Highly sensitive data (passwords, SSN, medical, financial) — never share.
PII
Personally Identifiable Information — data that could identify a specific person.
Passphrase
A long password made of multiple words — easier to remember and harder to crack.
Password manager
An app that stores your passwords securely so you don't reuse or write them down.
MFA / 2FA
Multi-factor authentication — logging in with TWO proofs (password + a code or device).

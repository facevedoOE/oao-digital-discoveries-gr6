Digital Wellness & Screen Time

For 13 weeks you've learned how the digital world works. This week, the focus turns inward: how screens affect your body, your mind, and your sleep — and how a 6th-grader can build a balance plan that actually sticks.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

1
Distinguish active and passive screen time and explain why the difference matters.
2
Describe the physical effects of screens on sleep, eyes, posture, and movement.
3
Recognize mental and emotional effects of screens, especially comparison and doomscrolling.
4
Build a personal balance plan using concrete habits — not vague resolutions.
01
Section 1 · The Screen Time Question

Not all screen time is created equal

The average American 8–12-year-old spends about 5.5 hours a day on screens — outside of school. That's roughly one third of waking hours. But the more useful question isn't how many hours; it's what kind of hours.

Researchers split screen time into two big buckets: active and passive. The same 60 minutes can feel very different — and affect you very differently — depending on which bucket it falls into.

⚡ Active Screen Time
Creating, learning, connecting.
  • Coding a project, drawing in an art app
  • Doing schoolwork on Canvas
  • Video-calling Grandma
  • Writing a story, editing a video
📺 Passive Screen Time
Scrolling, watching, absorbing.
  • Endless TikTok / Reels / Shorts scrolling
  • Background TV in your room
  • Auto-playing YouTube for hours
  • Doomscrolling through news headlines
📘 Vocabulary

Screen time — the total time spent looking at a digital screen.

Active screen time — using screens to create, learn, or connect with people you know.

Passive screen time — using screens mostly to consume content (watch, scroll) without producing or interacting.

02
Section 2 · What Screens Do to Your Body

Sleep, eyes, posture, and movement

Doctors and researchers around the world agree: heavy screen use changes how your body works. Most of the changes are small and reversible, but they add up — and four of them matter most for sixth-graders:

😴 Sleep
Blue light from screens tells your brain "it's daytime" and delays the sleep hormone melatonin. Phones in bed = trouble falling asleep AND lower-quality sleep.
👀 Eyes
Long screen sessions cause digital eye strain — dry eyes, blurry vision, headaches. The fix is the 20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds.
🧍 Posture
Hunching over a phone for hours strains your neck — sometimes called "tech neck." Sitting at a desk with a screen at eye-level helps a lot.
🏃 Movement
Sedentary behavior means sitting still for long stretches. The CDC recommends kids get at least 60 minutes of physical activity a day — screens often crowd that out.
✅ Check for Understanding

Pause and think: You're falling asleep with your phone in your hand and you wake up groggy every morning. Which physical effect from this section is the most likely culprit?

(Hint: it has to do with light, hormones, and bedtime.)

03
Section 3 · What Screens Do to Your Mind

Attention, mood, and the comparison trap

Screens don't just affect your body — they shape how you feel and what you pay attention to. Three patterns show up over and over in mental-health research:

🧠Attention & Focus
Notifications and infinite scroll train your brain to crave constant new things. After a lot of fast-cut video, longer tasks (reading, listening) can feel harder. The good news: attention is a muscle. Less scrolling = stronger focus.
📉The Comparison Trap
Social comparison — measuring yourself against others — is normal. But scrolling shows you everyone's highlight reel: best vacations, best outfits, best moments. Feeling worse about your own life after social media is one of the most studied effects in teen psychology.
🌪️Doomscrolling
Scrolling through one bad-news headline after another, often late at night. Your brain interprets each story as if it's happening to you — which can leave you feeling anxious without anything real being wrong.
💭 Think About It

The classical philosophers ranked temperance — self-control, the ability to enjoy a good thing in the right amount — as one of the four cardinal virtues. Screens aren't the enemy. They're a powerful good thing that needs measure. Asking "is this serving me, or am I serving it?" is temperance in 2025 form.

04
Section 4 · The Good Side of Screens

Screens are also where you learn, create, and connect

It's easy to make this whole topic sound like "screens bad." But that's not what the research actually says. Used wisely, screens are responsible for some of the biggest opportunities in human history — and your generation has more access than any before it.

📚 Learning
Free Khan Academy. Wikipedia in 300+ languages. Coding tutorials. Documentaries. Online tutoring.
🎨 Creating
Art apps, video editing, music production, writing tools, 3D design — all in your pocket.
💬 Connecting
Video-calling family far away, group chats with close friends, classmates collaborating on projects.
🌍 Exploring
Virtual museum tours, satellite views of any place on Earth, live nature cams from every continent.
💡 Did You Know?

Researchers find a "U-shaped curve" with screen time and well-being: a small amount can actually raise well-being (because it helps you connect and learn), but heavy use brings it back down. The sweet spot for most kids is moderate, intentional, mostly active screen time.

05
Section 5 · Build YOUR Balance Plan

Concrete habits beat vague resolutions

"I'll use my phone less" is a wish — and wishes don't change behavior. "I'll plug my phone in at 9 p.m. across the room" is a habit. Six concrete habits beat any number of fuzzy intentions:

1
Phone-free bedtime
Plug it in across the room at least 30 minutes before sleep. Use a real alarm clock if you need to.
2
Phone-free meals
Phones face-down or in another room during family meals. Eye contact and conversation matter.
3
20-20-20 for eyes
Every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds. Set a gentle timer to remind yourself.
4
Notification surgery
Turn off all notifications you don't actually need. Keep messages from people; mute apps that just want your attention.
5
Move every hour
Stand up, stretch, walk to another room. Aim for 60 minutes of physical activity total each day.
6
Active > passive
When you reach for a screen, ask: am I about to create or connect, or just consume? Not always wrong to consume — but notice the choice.
🌎 Real-World Connection

Many of the engineers who built today's most addictive apps now keep their own kids off them. They know exactly how the dopamine loop works — they designed it. Knowing how the system is built is the first step in choosing whether to play along.

Doctors, athletes, professional musicians, and elite students all share one habit: protected, focused time without a screen. Building that habit at 11 puts you years ahead.

📚 Key Vocabulary — Week 14

The total time spent looking at a digital screen.
Active screen time
Using screens to create, learn, or genuinely connect.
Passive screen time
Mostly consuming content (scrolling, watching) without producing or interacting.
Blue light
Wavelength of light that screens emit; can suppress melatonin and delay sleep.
Sedentary behavior
Sitting still for long stretches without physical activity.
20-20-20 rule
Every 20 minutes of screen use, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds.
Social comparison
Measuring yourself against others — heightened by social media's highlight reels.
Doomscrolling
Scrolling through one bad-news headline after another, often late at night.
Digital wellness
The practice of using technology in ways that support your physical and mental health.
Temperance
Classical virtue of self-control — enjoying good things in the right amount.

Cyber Safety PSA Project

For six weeks you've built a digital-safety toolkit. This week you'll teach what you've learned to someone who needs it most: a younger student. Choose a topic, pick a format, and create a Public Service Announcement (PSA) that could actually change a 4th-grader's online life.

The Project at a Glance

Four moves to make this project work:

1
Pick one cyber-safety topic from Weeks 9–14 — the one you think a younger student NEEDS to hear.
2
Choose a format — poster, infographic, slide deck, video, or comic strip.
3
Build a PSA with 4 parts — a hook, three accurate facts, a short story or example, and a call-to-action.
4
Submit to Canvas with the planning workbook and a 3-sentence designer's note.
01
Section 1 · What's a PSA?

A short, sharp message designed to change behavior

A Public Service Announcement is a piece of communication built to inform the public about something important — usually a safety issue, a health risk, or a smart habit. PSAs are why you know "Stop, Drop, and Roll." They're why you've heard "Click It or Ticket." They work because they're short, memorable, and aimed at a specific person.

A great PSA respects the audience's time. It doesn't lecture — it shows. It doesn't list 50 things — it focuses on one big idea and three sharp facts. By the end, the audience knows exactly what to do differently tomorrow.

📘 Project Vocabulary

PSA (Public Service Announcement) — a short message designed to inform and change behavior on an important topic.

Audience — the specific person you're making the PSA for.

Hook — the first 5 seconds that make someone stop scrolling.

Call-to-action (CTA) — the one specific thing you want the audience to do.

Section 3 · Pick Your Format

Five formats — pick the one that fits YOU

There's no "best" format — there's the one that lets you communicate clearly and that you'll actually finish on time. Each format has its own strengths:

📰Poster (1 page)
Made in Canva, Google Slides, or even on paper. Strength: sharp, focused, easy to scan. Watch for: too much text — be ruthless.
📊Infographic
A poster organized around statistics or a process. Strength: data tells the story. Watch for: always cite where your facts came from.
🎬Video (60–90 seconds)
Recorded with a phone, animated in CapCut/iMovie/Canva. Strength: emotion, voice, story. Watch for: keep it short and write a script first.
📑Slide Deck (5–8 slides)
Google Slides or PowerPoint. Strength: clear progression and room for visuals. Watch for: one big idea per slide — don't paste paragraphs.
💬Comic Strip (3–6 panels)
Drawn or made with a comic tool. Strength: story is built in. Watch for: the punchline IS your call-to-action.
💭 Think About It

A great communicator picks the format that serves the message — not the one that seems most impressive. Classical rhetoric calls this aptness: matching the form to the moment. A 90-second video can be brilliant, but a single sharp poster sometimes lands harder. Choose for impact, not for showing off.

Section 4 · The 4 Parts of a Strong PSA

Hook → Facts → Story → Call-to-Action

Every effective PSA — whether it's a Super Bowl ad or a 4th-grade poster — has the same four moves. Build all four into your project and you're already in B+ territory.

1
HOOK — Make them stop scrolling
A 1-sentence question, surprising fact, or image that grabs attention in under 3 seconds. Examples: "Your phone is awake while you sleep — is that okay?" / "It takes a computer 1 second to crack 'password123.'"
2
FACTS — Three accurate, sourced claims
Three real facts from your topic. Cite where each one came from (lesson reading, .gov page, expert source). This is where the CRAAP test from Week 13 pays off.
3
STORY or EXAMPLE — Make the facts feel real
A short scenario (real or invented) showing the issue in action. People remember stories about people far better than they remember statistics on their own.
4
CALL-TO-ACTION — One specific habit they can start tomorrow
Not "be safe online" (vague). Try "Plug your phone in across the room every night" or "Never click a link in a text from a number you don't know." Concrete habits beat fuzzy advice.
💡 Did You Know?

The most effective PSA in U.S. history — the "Click It or Ticket" seatbelt campaign — added one number, one image, and one clear action. That's it. Seatbelt use went up roughly 15 percentage points nationwide. Simple beats fancy when the message is sharp.

Section 5 · The Process & Rubric

A 4-day plan that actually fits in a school week

📅 Recommended Schedule
DAY 1
Plan. Open the planning workbook, pick topic, format, audience. Write your hook draft.
DAY 2
Research. Find your three facts using CRAAP-quality sources. Write them down with citations.
DAY 3
Build. Make your poster, video, slides, infographic, or comic. Get all four parts in.
DAY 4
Revise & Submit. Run the checklist. Show it to one person and ask if the call-to-action is clear. Submit to Canvas with your designer's note.

📊 You'll Be Graded On 4 Criteria

CONTENT ACCURACY
Facts are correct and tied to a real Unit 2 standard.
CRAFT & CLARITY
Visuals, language, and layout serve the message.
AUDIENCE FIT
A 4th-grader could understand and act on it.
CITATION & SOURCING
Facts cite where they came from (CRAAP-passed).

PSAs are how the U.S. Surgeon General, the FDA, the CDC, and most state governments talk to the public about health and safety. The same four-part structure you'll use this week is taught in journalism schools, ad agencies, and government communication offices.

Your project is small — but it's real practice in the same craft adults use to keep their communities safe.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q1. Which of the following is the BEST definition of phishing?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) A type of fishing app for your phone
     ❌ Phishing is NOT a real fishing app — the spelling
        comes from a hacker term, but it's a scam, not a sport.
  B) A scam that uses fake messages to trick people into
     giving up passwords or personal info                   ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Phishing uses fake messages — emails, texts, DMs —
        to trick people into handing over secret info.
        (SC.6.CS.1.3)
  C) A virus that damages computer hardware
     ❌ That describes a hardware-damaging virus. Phishing
        tricks PEOPLE — not the hardware itself.
  D) A way to back up your files to the cloud
     ❌ That's cloud backup — a totally different topic and
        not a scam.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q2. Which piece of information should be classified as
    CONFIDENTIAL — never shared with anyone online?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Your favorite music genre
     ❌ Music tastes are PUBLIC — totally fine to share.
  B) The name of your school
     ❌ A school name is generally PUBLIC — printed on
        jerseys, posted on websites.
  C) Your password and the 6-digit MFA code that just texted
     to your phone                                          ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Passwords and MFA codes are CONFIDENTIAL. Real
        companies will NEVER ask for them — anyone who does
        is trying to scam you. (SC.6.CS.1.4)
  D) Your favorite sports team
     ❌ Sports preferences are PUBLIC — already visible on
        your profile or already known.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q3. Which of these is the STRONGEST password?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) password123
     ❌ One of the most common passwords in the world. A
        computer cracks it in under a second.
  B) qwerty
     ❌ The top 5 letters on a keyboard. Computers try this
        in their first 100 guesses.
  C) orange-lamp-tiger-cloud-9                              ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ A long passphrase with random words and a number is
        hard to guess and would take longer than the age of
        the universe to brute-force. (SC.6.CS.2.1)
  D) your first name + the year you were born
     ❌ Way too easy to guess if a phisher knows even a
        little about you. Avoid name + birthdate combos.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q4. What does multi-factor authentication (MFA) protect you
    against, even if a scammer steals your password?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Slow internet speeds
     ❌ MFA isn't about speed — it's about identity.
  B) Account takeover by an attacker who has only your
     password                                               ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Even if a phisher steals your password, they can't
        log in without the second factor (a code on your
        phone). (SC.6.CS.2.1)
  C) Lost device damage and broken screens
     ❌ MFA doesn't protect physical devices — it protects
        access to your accounts.
  D) Battery drain on your phone
     ❌ MFA has nothing to do with battery life.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q5. You're at a coffee shop on free public Wi-Fi. Which
    activity is the LEAST safe to do on this network?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Reading a public news article
     ❌ Reading public news is low-risk on any network —
        you're not exposing private credentials.
  B) Watching a YouTube video
     ❌ Watching public videos is low-risk — no private
        logins involved.
  C) Logging into your bank account and transferring money  ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Banking and other sensitive logins on public Wi-Fi
        can be intercepted. Wait until you're on a trusted
        network. (SC.6.HS.1.1)
  D) Checking the weather
     ❌ Looking up public info isn't risky. The danger is
        logging into accounts with sensitive data.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q1. You want your dad to READ your essay but NOT change any of
    your sentences. Which role should you give him?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Owner
     ❌ Owner has full control — including delete and re-share.
        Way more than needed for "just read it."
  B) Editor
     ❌ Editor can change your sentences. You said you don't
        want him doing that.
  C) Viewer                                                ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Viewer is read-only — he can open and read but cannot
        change anything. (SC.6.CS.1.2)
  D) Commenter
     ❌ Commenter could leave comments on your file. If you
        only want him to read silently, Viewer is the tighter
        choice.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q2. Your teacher wants to leave feedback on your draft but
    should NOT rewrite your sentences. Which role gives her
    exactly what she needs?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Editor
     ❌ Editor can rewrite your sentences directly. The
        question said she shouldn't.
  B) Viewer
     ❌ Viewer is read-only — she couldn't leave feedback at
        all.
  C) Commenter                                             ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Commenter can read and leave comments or suggestions,
        but cannot change the actual content. (SC.6.CS.1.2)
  D) Owner
     ❌ Owner has full control of the file, including delete.
        Far too much access for a feedback role.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q3. What is the PRINCIPLE OF LEAST PRIVILEGE?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Always give every user the highest possible role so
     nothing breaks
     ❌ That's the OPPOSITE of least privilege — and a recipe
        for accidents and leaks.
  B) Give every user exactly the access they need to do their
     job — and nothing more                                ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Match the role to the real need — no extra access. The
        fewer keys floating around, the safer your data.
        (SC.6.CS.1.2)
  C) Only the IT admin should ever have access to anything
     ❌ That would block real work. Least privilege means
        right-sizing access, not eliminating it.
  D) Default to "Anyone with the link" so people don't have
     to ask
     ❌ That's the most-privilege setting, not least
        privilege. It's the kind of mistake that causes real-
        world data leaks.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q4. Which sharing setting is the MOST risky for a private
    file you don't want strangers to see?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Specific people, role: Viewer
     ❌ Very safe — only the people you list can find it, and
        they can only read.
  B) Anyone in your school, role: Viewer
     ❌ Some risk, since everyone in your school can find it
        — but the public still can't.
  C) Anyone with the link, role: Editor                   ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ The most dangerous combo — anyone who gets the link
        can not only read but also change your file.
        (SC.6.CS.1.2)
  D) Specific people, role: Editor
     ❌ Reasonable for a small trusted group — only the named
        people can find it, even if they can edit.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Q5. Your sister leaves her laptop open and signed into her
    email. You see a private message from her teacher about
    her grades. What is the right thing to do?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A) Read the message — her email is right there in front of
     you
     ❌ Just because you CAN see it doesn't mean you SHOULD.
        That's the whole idea of ethical access.
  B) Look away, close the laptop, and let her know it was
     open                                                  ✓ CORRECT
     ✅ Treat other people's private data the way you'd want
        them to treat yours — close the door and walk away.
        (SC.6.CS.1.2)
  C) Read it and screenshot it, in case it's useful later
     ❌ Even worse — now you've made a copy of someone else's
        private data without permission.
  D) Reply to the teacher on her behalf to be helpful
     ❌ You'd be impersonating her — and using her account
        without permission. Big trust break.
